# Bundle: The Language of the City

This bundle explores urban vocabulary, spatial navigation, and the pragmatic interpretation of signs and instructions in public spaces, ranging from simple location identification to complex semiotic analysis of the urban environment.

---

## Question 1 (Place Identification - Difficulty 1)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-places-001-PRO-v1`
**Type:** `single`
**ICFES:** Lexical
**Bloom:** Remember

I need to buy some medicine because I have a headache.

### Enunciado
Where should I go?

### Options
- [x] A) To the drugstore / pharmacy
- [ ] B) To the bakery
- [ ] C) To the park
- [ ] D) To the fire station

### Explicación Pedagógica
Las medicinas se compran en la droguería o farmacia (drugstore/pharmacy). Las otras opciones venden pan, son para recreación o para emergencias de fuego.

## Question 16 (Semiotic Interpretation - Difficulty 6)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-places-001-PRO-v16`
**Type:** `multi-correct`
**ICFES:** Comunicativa + Pragmática
**Bloom:** Analyze+

### Contexto
Signs like 'No Loitering' or 'Private Property - No Trespassing' in public-looking squares are examples of 'Privately Owned Public Spaces' (POPS). These spaces look public but are governed by private rules, creating a tension between visual accessibility and legal restriction.

### Enunciado
Which of the following describes the nature of POPS according to the text? (Select ALL correct options)

### Options
- [x] A) They feature a conflict between appearance and legal reality. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [ ] B) They are completely illegal and should be demolished.
- [x] C) They use signage to enforce private behavioral standards in open areas. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] D) They can deceive users into thinking they have more rights than they actually do. <!-- weight: 0.8 -->
- [ ] E) They are always free of any rules or restrictions.

### Scoring
- Correct: A, C, D.
- B and E are not supported by the text.

### Explicación Pedagógica
El texto resalta la tensión entre lo visual (público) y lo legal (privado) (A), el uso de avisos para reglas privadas (C) y el potencial engaño sobre los derechos del usuario (D).

## Question 17 (Urban Design Criticism - Difficulty 7)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-places-001-PRO-v17`
**Type:** `multi-correct`
**ICFES:** Comunicativa + Lexical
**Bloom:** Evaluate

### Contexto
'Hostile Architecture' refers to urban design elements like slanted benches or spikes in doorways intended to prevent people from sleeping or staying too long in public places. Critics argue this removes the 'humanity' of the city.

### Enunciado
What characterizes 'Hostile Architecture'? (Select ALL correct options)

### Options
- [x] A) Design choices that prioritize control over comfort. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) Using physical objects to discourage specific social behaviors. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [ ] C) Creating more spaces for the homeless to rest.
- [x] D) A subtle form of social exclusion built into the environment. <!-- weight: 0.8 -->
- [ ] E) Using bright colors to make the city look more friendly.

### Scoring
- Correct: A, B, D.
- C is the opposite of its goal. E is not mentioned and contradicts the 'hostile' nature.

### Explicación Pedagógica
La arquitectura hostil prioriza el control (A), desalienta conductas sociales (B) y es una forma de exclusión (D).

## Question 18 (Socio-Economic Synthesis - Difficulty 8)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-places-001-PRO-v18`
**Type:** `weighted`
**ICFES:** Pragmática + Comunicativa
**Bloom:** Create

### Contexto
Concept 1: 'Gentrification'—the process of renovating a poor neighborhood so that it conforms to middle-class taste, often displacing original residents.
Concept 2: 'Urban Renewal'—the improvement of city infrastructure, lighting, and safety.

### Enunciado
Synthesize the ethical dilemma of improving a city neighborhood:

### Options
- [x] A) Urban improvement is a moral paradox: the same actions that increase safety and value can simultaneously destroy the cultural identity and affordability for long-term residents. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) Improving a neighborhood is always good, and those who can't afford it should just leave. <!-- weight: 0.4 -->
- [x] C) We should never repair anything in a city to avoid the risk of displacement. <!-- weight: 0.2 -->
- [ ] D) Safety and culture have no relationship at all in urban planning. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring
- A is the most sophisticated synthesis of the dilemma (1.0).
- B and C represent extreme, non-inclusive views (0.4 and 0.2).

### Explicación Pedagógica
La opción A reconoce que la mejora (seguridad/valor) tiene un costo social (identidad/asequibilidad), capturando la complejidad del problema.

## Question 19 (Meta-environmental Paradox - Difficulty 9)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-places-001-PRO-v19`
**Type:** `weighted`
**ICFES:** Meta-cognitive
**Bloom:** Meta-cognitive

### Contexto
The 'Walkability Paradox' suggests that as a city becomes more walkable and pleasant for pedestrians (fewer cars, more parks), property values rise so much that the average person can no longer afford to live there, forcing them to move far away where they MUST use a car to commute.

### Enunciado
What is the logical flaw in a city's 'green' strategy that ignores the Walkability Paradox?

### Options
- [x] A) It creates a local environmental success (a beautiful park) at the cost of a global environmental failure (increased car travel for displaced workers). <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) It makes people too healthy, which is bad for the economy and hospitals. <!-- weight: 0.2 -->
- [x] C) It is better to have cars everywhere so that everyone can afford to live in the city center. <!-- weight: 0.4 -->
- [ ] D) Parks have no impact on house prices; people hate trees. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring
- A identifies the meta-scale logical flaw (Local vs Global) (1.0).
- C is a common but flawed urbanist counter-argument (0.4).

### Explicación Pedagógica
La paradoja reside en que lo que parece "verde" localmente puede causar más daño "globalmente" si desplaza a la gente, lo cual es capturado perfectamente en A.

## Question 20 (Abstract System Transfer - Difficulty 10)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-places-001-PRO-v20`
**Type:** `weighted`
**ICFES:** Todas
**Bloom:** Transfer+

### Contexto
In computer science, a 'User Interface' (UI) is how a human interacts with a system. In biology, 'Niche Construction' is the process by which an organism alters its environment to improve its survival.

### Enunciado
How can the 'infrastructure' of a city be viewed as both a 'Societal UI' and 'Species Niche Construction'?

### Options
- [x] A) Infrastructure acts as the interface through which citizens access social services and economic opportunity, while simultaneously representing the unique way the human species has altered the Earth to facilitate its own collective survival. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) A city is just a bunch of buildings and has nothing to do with biology or computers. <!-- weight: 0.1 -->
- [x] C) Cities are mistakes that humans made because they wanted to be different from animals. <!-- weight: 0.3 -->
- [ ] D) Computers designed cities, so they are the same thing. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring
- A demonstrates extraordinary transfer by linking infrastructure (Civil Engineering) to UI (Software) and Niche Construction (Biology/Evolution) (1.0).
- C and B are reductive and lack interdisciplinary depth.

### Explicación Pedagógica
Esta pregunta requiere que el estudiante vea la ciudad no como cemento, sino como un sistema de interacción (UI) y como una adaptación biológica masiva (Niche Construction).
