## Master Context A: The Dawn of the Machines (Questions 1-10)

Before the 18th century, the rhythm of life was dictated by the sun and the muscle of animals. The Industrial Revolution, however, didn't just introduce machines; it rewired the human brain’s relationship with time and space. When James Watt perfected the steam engine, he unknowingly signed the death warrant of the traditional agrarian village.

Factories required thousands of workers in single locations, leading to the rapid birth of urban centers like Manchester. These cities grew without planning, under the thick soot of coal. While the Victorian middle class celebrated "progress" and "innovation," the working class experienced this shift as a loss of autonomy. They were no longer masters of their own tools; they were extensions of a metal apparatus that never slept. Today, we look back at this era as the engine of modern wealth, but for those living in the smog, it was often a terrifying transition from being a "someone" in a village to a "number" in a ledger.

## Master Context B: The Global Village (Questions 11-20)

In 1962, Marshall McLuhan coined the term "Global Village" to describe how electronics would shrink the world. Decades later, we live in that village. Globalization is not merely a trading agreement; it is an ontological shift. When a teenager in Medellin listens to K-pop while wearing shoes designed in Portland and manufactured in Vietnam, the concept of "national culture" becomes increasingly porous.

Supporters argue that globalization is the most powerful weapon against poverty, spreading technology and lifting millions into the consumer class. However, critics see it as "cultural imperialism," where a handful of global corporations homogenize human experience. If everyone watches the same movies and uses the same apps, do we lose the local "secret ingredients" of our identity? The challenge of the 21st century is the "Global-Local Paradox": how to participate in the planetary network without erasing the unique historical footprint of our ancestors.
