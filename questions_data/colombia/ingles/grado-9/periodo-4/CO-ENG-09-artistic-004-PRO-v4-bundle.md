## Master Context A: The Muralists of Latin America (Questions 1-10)

In the early 20th century, a group of artists in Mexico decided that art should not stay inside museums for the rich. They started painting giant murals on the walls of public government buildings. Figures like Diego Rivera used vibrant colors to tell the history of indigenous people and the struggles of workers.

Murals were meant to be "books for those who couldn't read." By using visual symbols, artists explained complex social transformations to the general public. Rivera’s work often included industrial scenes, showing his fascination with the machines of the North, but always centered on the human face. This movement influenced street art globally, turning city walls into permanent political stages where history is kept alive for everyone.

## Master Context B: Jazz and the Voice of Freedom (Questions 11-20)

Jazz was born in New Orleans as a fusion of African rhythms and European harmonies. For many black Americans in the early 20th century, jazz was not just music; it was a form of cultural resistance. In a time of segregation, jazz clubs were one of the few places where shared creativity broke racial barriers.

Louis Armstrong, one of the pioneers, used his trumpet to "speak" when words were forbidden. The improvisation in jazz symbolized personal freedom within a collective structure. Unlike classical music, jazz allowed individual players to lead and change the melody in real-time. This spirit of "breaking the rules" made jazz a global symbol of liberation, spreading from the United States to Europe and beyond, influencing civil rights movements and modern pop culture.
