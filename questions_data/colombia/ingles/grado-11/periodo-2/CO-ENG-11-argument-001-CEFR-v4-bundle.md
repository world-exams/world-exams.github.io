## 📖 Model Essay A: Should Universities Make Education Free? (Questions 1–10)

**Introduction:** The question of whether higher education should be publicly funded and free at the point of access has become one of the most contested in contemporary education policy. In countries like Germany and Norway, tuition-free university education has been a reality for decades. Yet critics argue that this model is unsustainable, inequitable, and misaligns incentives. I will argue that free higher education represents not a utopian aspiration but a pragmatic social investment whose long-term benefits outweigh its costs — provided it is implemented alongside robust university funding mechanisms.

**Argument 1 — Economic Returns:** Education is among the highest-return public investments available to governments. Research from the OECD consistently shows that each additional year of tertiary education correlates with a 7-10% increase in lifetime earnings and a corresponding increase in tax revenue. A graduate's additional lifetime tax contribution frequently exceeds the cost of their subsidized education over a 20-30 year period, making free education not a giveaway but a deferred return on public investment.

**Argument 2 — Equity:** Tuition fees create a structural barrier to higher education for students from lower-income families, regardless of intellectual ability. Even with loan systems, debt-aversion among low-income families disproportionately reduces their enrollment rates. Free tuition removes this psychological and financial barrier, enabling a meritocratic allocation of educational opportunity.

**Counterargument and Refutation:** Critics argue that free universities deliver lower quality education, citing evidence from some transition periods. However, this correlation conflates cause and effect. University quality is a function of funding adequacy, not tuition source. Germany's world-class research universities demonstrate that high quality and free access are compatible when governments fund institutions directly rather than routing money through student loans.

**Conclusion:** Free higher education is not a luxury for wealthy nations; it is an investment in human capital and social cohesion available to any government that chooses to prioritize it. The question is not whether society can afford it but whether it is willing to allocate resources accordingly.

## 📖 Model Essay B: On the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence (Questions 11–20)

**Introduction:** Artificial intelligence is no longer a speculative technology; it is already embedded in decisions that determine who receives a loan, who is prioritized for medical care, and who is recommended for parole. As AI systems acquire greater decision-making authority over human lives, the ethics of their deployment becomes an urgent political, not merely technical, matter. This essay argues that meaningful AI governance requires not just technical regulation but a fundamental rethinking of accountability, transparency, and democratic oversight in the design of algorithmic systems.

**Core Argument:** Current AI systems are often described as "black boxes" — their decision-making processes are opaque even to their creators. When an algorithm denies a mortgage or flags a patient as low-priority, no clear mechanism exists to challenge that decision or even understand it. This violates a fundamental principle of justice: the right to know the reasons for decisions that affect your life. The opacity of these systems is not a technical inevitability but a design choice made in contexts where commercial imperatives have overridden democratic accountability.

**Counterargument:** Proponents of the status quo argue that AI decisions are more objective than human judgments, which are subject to unconscious bias, fatigue, and inconsistency. If algorithms perform better on measurable outcomes (lower loan default rates, more accurate disease diagnoses), why impose transparency requirements that may reduce their efficiency?

**Refutation:** This argument mistakes correlation for causation and efficiency for justice. An algorithm trained on biased historical data will reproduce and amplify those biases with mathematical precision — the appearance of objectivity masks systematic discrimination. Furthermore, efficiency and accountability are not mutually exclusive. Explainable AI (XAI) research demonstrates that transparent models can match or approach the accuracy of opaque ones in many contexts.
