**Options:**
- [ ] A) "Significant" always means the same as "considerable" in academic English.
- [x] B) "Significant" in scientific writing often implies statistical significance (p-value) — a specific technical claim; "considerable" means notably large in magnitude but without implying statistical testing.

## Question 20 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 10
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-science-reading-002-CEFR-v20`
**Skill:** C1 meta-linguistic awareness — function of hedging in science

**Enunciado:** A student asks: "Why do scientists use so many hedging words? Doesn't it make their arguments weaker?" Choose the response that demonstrates C1-level understanding:

**Options:**
- [x] A) Hedging signals epistemic precision — the language of probability and conditionality calibrates claims to the actual strength of evidence. Claiming more certainty than evidence warrants is a form of scientific dishonesty; calibrated uncertainty is a mark of intellectual integrity, not weakness.
- [ ] B) Scientists use hedging because they are afraid of being wrong and do not want to commit to their findings.
- [ ] C) Hedging is only used in humanities; in natural sciences, all claims are stated definitively.
- [ ] D) Hedging makes language weaker but scientists use it because academic journals require it.
