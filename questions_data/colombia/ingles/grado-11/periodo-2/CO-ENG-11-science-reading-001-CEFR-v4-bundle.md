## 📖 Master Context A: The Replication Crisis in Science (Questions 1–10)

In 2011, a team of psychologists at Bayer HealthCare reviewed their internal replication attempts of published academic findings and found that only 25% of pre-clinical studies could be successfully reproduced. A landmark 2015 project by the Open Science Collaboration attempted to replicate 100 published psychological studies and succeeded with only approximately 39%. These findings shocked the scientific community and launched what became known as the **replication crisis** — a recognition that a significant proportion of peer-reviewed scientific results may not hold up when subjected to independent verification.

The causes are multiple and structural. **Publication bias** plays a central role: academic journals have historically preferred to publish positive results (findings that confirm hypotheses) over null results (studies that found no effect). This creates a systematically skewed published literature in which failed studies are invisible. Additionally, a competitive publish-or-perish academic culture incentivizes researchers to engage in **p-hacking** — analyzing data in multiple ways until a statistically significant result is found — and **HARKing** (Hypothesizing After Results are Known), where researchers write up their hypothesis as if it had been specified before the study despite being generated by the data.

The implications extend beyond academic integrity. Many evidence-based policy decisions — in nutrition, psychology, education, and medicine — rest on published studies that may not replicate. A nutrition guideline recommending reduced fat intake, or a school intervention program claimed to improve outcomes, may have a weaker empirical foundation than assumed.

The scientific community has responded with open science initiatives: pre-registration (declaring study design and hypotheses before data collection), mandatory publication of null results, and data-sharing requirements. While these reforms represent genuine progress, critics note that structural incentives in academia — career advancement tied to novel, positive findings — have not fundamentally changed.

## 📖 Master Context B: Reading a Scientific Abstract (Questions 11–20)

*Students at B2-C1 must be able to read and extract information from scientific abstracts. The following is an authentic-style abstract:*

---

**ABSTRACT: The Effect of Urban Green Space on Adolescent Mental Health Outcomes: A Longitudinal Cohort Study**

**Background:** Urbanization has been associated with elevated rates of anxiety and depression in adolescent populations. While cross-sectional studies have suggested a positive association between proximity to green space and improved mental health, causal inference has been limited by methodological constraints.

**Objective:** To determine whether an increase in residential green space is causally associated with improvements in self-reported anxiety and depression scores among urban adolescents over a 24-month period.

**Methods:** A prospective longitudinal cohort of 2,847 urban adolescents (ages 12–18) was followed over 24 months. Participants completed validated psychological measures (GAD-7, PHQ-9) at baseline and at 12 and 24 months. Green space proximity was measured using satellite imagery (NDVI index). Regression analysis was used to control for socioeconomic status, baseline mental health, and demographics.

**Results:** After controlling for confounders, a 10% increase in green space within a 500m residential radius was associated with a statistically significant reduction in GAD-7 scores (β = -0.42, p < 0.001) and PHQ-9 scores (β = -0.38, p < 0.01). Effect sizes were modest but consistent across demographic subgroups.

**Conclusions:** These findings provide longitudinal evidence of a modest but statistically significant causal association between increased urban green space and improved adolescent mental health, with implications for urban planning and public health policy. Replication in diverse urban contexts is recommended before policy generalization.
