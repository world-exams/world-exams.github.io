## Reading Text 1: The Ontology of the Multiverse
For the majority of scientific history, cosmology operated under a singular, uncompromising paradigm: the Universe encompasses all that physically exists. However, advancements in quantum mechanics and string theory have increasingly destabilized this monolothic worldview, giving rise to the controversial "Multiverse" hypothesis. In its various iterations—from the "Many-Worlds" interpretation of quantum mechanics to the inflationary bubble theory—the Multiverse proposes that our observable universe is merely one localized pocket within an infinite, eternal fractal of distinct cosmic domains, each potentially governed by entirely different physical constants.

While mathematically elegant, the Multiverse hypothesis has fractured the physics community, primarily due to its devastating implications for the scientific method. At its core, empirical science relies on falsifiability: a theory must be testable and capable of being proven wrong through observation. If parallel universes are causally disconnected from our own, and therefore fundamentally unobservable, the Multiverse hypothesis cannot be empirically verified. Critics argue that accepting unobservable phenomena as scientific fact effectively drags physics backward into the realm of metaphysics and theology. If physicists can "explain away" the highly specific, life-permitting constants of our universe simply by arguing that "in an infinite multiverse, every possibility must exist somewhere," they risk abandoning the pursuit of profound, underlying physical laws in favor of a lazy anthropic tautology.

## Reading Text 2: The Attention Economy
In the digital epoch, human attention has superseded oil, data, and currency as the global economy's most contested and lucrative commodity. Unlike material resources, attention is fundamentally zero-sum and biologically finite; an individual possesses precisely twenty-four hours in a day. Consequently, social media conglomerates do not merely build platforms to facilitate connection; they engineer highly sophisticated digital environments designed to hijack human psychology and maximize "time-on-device." To achieve this, algorithms are calibrated to exploit evolutionary vulnerabilities, optimizing content delivery to trigger consistent dopamine release.

The social fallout of this hyper-optimized attention extraction has been catastrophic. Because algorithms quickly determine that outrage, indignation, and tribal fear generate the highest engagement metrics—far outpacing nuance, empathy, or factual reporting—they systematically amplify extreme content. This creates algorithmic echo chambers that radicalize users and fracture civic discourse. More perniciously, the constant barrage of micro-stimuli shatters our capacity for deep, sustained focus, inducing a state of chronic cognitive fragmentation. The modern user is thus trapped in a paradox: hyper-connected to global information streams, yet neurologically starved of the uninterrupted silence required for profound thought, self-reflection, or meaningful human connection.

## Reading Text 3: The Epigenetic Revolution
For most of the 20th century, genetic deterministic theory reigned supreme. The scientific consensus posited that DNA was an unalterable blueprint—a rigid, hermetically sealed biological destiny. You inherited your genes, and those genes dictated your traits, susceptibilities, and physical fate, largely impervious to external influence. However, the emergence of epigenetics has utterly revolutionized this deterministic paradigm. Epigenetics is the study of how behaviors and environment can cause changes that affect the way your genes work.

Crucially, epigenetic changes do not alter the underlying DNA sequence; instead, they alter how your body reads that sequence. Chemical compounds, such as methyl groups, can attach to genes and effectively turn them "on" or "off." What makes epigenetics profoundly paradigm-shifting is the discovery that these chemical tags are highly responsive to environmental stimuli—diet, chronic stress, environmental toxins, and even psychological trauma. Startlingly, studies on the descendants of trauma survivors, such as those who endured severe famines or the Holocaust, suggest that trauma-induced epigenetic modifications can be inherited across multiple generations. This implies that biological inheritance is not merely the transmission of static code, but the dynamic transmission of ancestral lived experience, permanently reframing our understanding of human malleability and generational responsibility.

### Enunciado
The Great Filter theory posits that technological progress fundamentally contains the seeds of its own sudden, catastrophic end. Which classical myth serves as the most accurate allegory for mankind facing a Great Filter located in its future?

### Options
- [x] A) The Myth of Icarus: Humanity's brilliant technological invention (wings of wax/nuclear physics) grants incredible power, but hubristic overreach guarantees a fatal downfall. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) The Myth of Sisyphus: Humanity is doomed to constantly build up technological civilizations only to watch them pointlessly collapse, over and over again for eternity. <!-- weight: 0.6 -->
- [x] C) Pandora's Box: By acquiring forbidden knowledge (advanced AI/nukes), humanity accidentally unleashes uncontrollable evils that destroy them, with only hope remaining. <!-- weight: 0.3 -->
- [ ] D) King Midas: Humanity gains the power to turn everything to gold, resulting in infinite wealth but starvation. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring
- Best answer (A): 1.0 point
- Second best (B): 0.6 points
- Acceptable (C): 0.3 points
- Incorrect (D): 0.0 points

### Explicación Pedagógica
The Great Filter (when placed in the future) implies that the very tools of our ascent are the tools of our destruction, driven by technological hubris. Icarus (A) perfectly mirrors this: the technology works, lifting him up, but his ambition causes the technology to fail, resulting in total destruction. Sisyphus (B) works for the "inevitable collapse" aspect but lacks the technological hubris angle. Pandora (C) works for "unleashing forbidden tech" but usually implies survival/hope, whereas the Filter implies total extinction. Midas (D) is about greed, not extinction-level technological barriers.
