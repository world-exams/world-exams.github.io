## 📖 Master Context A: Original Dystopian Extract — "The Ministry of Clarity" (Questions 1–10)

*Citizens were permitted to speak, of course. The Ministry had not abolished speech. It had simply reformed it. Words that produced what the Ministry termed "Clarity Hazards" — confusion, doubt, dissatisfaction — had been gradually retired from use. First the newspapers, then the schools, then everyday speech.*

*Mara had been eight years old when the word "freedom" was reclassified as a technical term requiring a permit. She was twelve when "unfair" was listed as a mental health indicator. By the time she was thirty, she found she could no longer quite remember what it had meant to wonder.*

*"Are you well?" a colleague asked her one morning.*

*"I am optimal," Mara replied. And she was — she was certain of it. The certainty was the most comfortable feeling in the world.*

## 📖 Master Context B: Editorial on Dystopian Literature as a Genre (Questions 11–20)

*Dystopian fiction has experienced remarkable resurgences at moments of acute political anxiety. The 1940s produced Orwell; the 1950s, Bradbury; the 2010s, a wave of young adult dystopian series. Critics who dismiss the genre as escapist fantasy misread its fundamental mechanism. Dystopia is not an escape from the present but an amplification of it — a thought experiment that asks: if this tendency continues unchecked, what might we become?*

*The genre's peculiar power lies in its dual address. It speaks simultaneously to the universal anxieties of any reader and to the specific pathologies of its historical moment. When we read Orwell's account of surveillance, we are simultaneously responding to timeless human fears about loss of privacy AND to the specific surveillance apparatus of the 1940s-1950s Cold War state. Contemporary readers bring their smartphones to that reading — and feel the text press differently against their experience.*

*Perhaps most importantly, dystopian fiction invites readers to "defamiliarize" their own world — to see the familiar as strange, the normal as potentially abnormal. This critical estrangement is, arguably, the most valuable civic function literature performs.*
