## Section 4: Advanced Editorial Grammar

## Question 14 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 8
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-editorial-002-CEFR-v14`
**Skill:** Stance adverbials — expressing certainty levels

**Enunciado:** Rank these sentences from MOST to LEAST certain stance: A) "Arguably, this is the most consequential policy failure of the decade." B) "Undoubtedly, the failure of voluntary mechanisms has been demonstrated." C) "It is possible that stronger binding mechanisms could produce better outcomes."

**Options:**
- [x] A) B (undoubtedly — highest certainty) → A (arguably — moderately certain/invites debate) → C (possible — low certainty/tentative).
- [ ] B) A → C → B from most to least certain.
- [ ] C) All three stance adverbials express the same degree of certainty.
- [ ] D) C → A → B from most to least certain.

## Question 20 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 10
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-editorial-002-CEFR-v20`
**Skill:** Meta-linguistic — why grammar matters in argument

**Enunciado:** An editor tells a student: "Your argument is correct but your grammar is weak — and that IS the editorial." Why is grammatical precision not separate from argumentative quality in formal writing?

**Options:**
- [x] A) In formal writing, grammar structures the precision of claims — relative clause placement determines what is essential vs. additional information; stance adverbials calibrate certainty; cleft sentences direct emphasis to the right argument. A grammatically weak sentence cannot carry a complex argument without ambiguity, and ambiguity is the enemy of persuasion.
- [ ] B) Grammar is purely aesthetic in editorial writing — the argument can be equally strong with basic grammatical structures.
- [ ] C) Grammar only matters for academic papers; editorials are judged solely on the strength of ideas.
- [ ] D) Good grammar is important because editors will reject badly written editorials for commercial reasons.
