## 📖 Poem A: "Two Languages" — Original poem (Questions 1–10)

*In Spanish*
*I dream in color.*
*In English*
*I file my taxes*
*and apologize for things*
*that are not my fault.*

*At the border*
*between them*
*I fall asleep*
*in the language*
*of my grandmother*
*and wake up*
*in the language*
*of my landlord.*

## Question 3 — **CEFR: B2** | Difficulty: 6
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-poetry-001-CEFR-v3`
**Skill:** Inference — "grandmother's language" vs. "landlord's language"

**Enunciado:** The poem ends with the speaker falling asleep in "the language of my grandmother" and waking in "the language of my landlord." What does this contrast imply?

**Options:**
- [x] A) The grandmother represents heritage, warmth, and emotional authenticity; the landlord represents the power relationship of the host country — economic dependency and formal transaction. Sleep/dreaming belongs to the private self; waking belongs to public obligation. The poem maps the border between languages onto the border between private and public selves.
- [ ] B) The speaker rents a room from someone who speaks English and the grandmother is Spanish-speaking — this is a realistic biographical detail.
- [ ] C) The landlord is a more important figure in the speaker's life than the grandmother.
- [ ] D) The poem is about the difficulty of maintaining good relationships with both family and landlords.

The poem suggests that the real border experience is not crossing a line on a map but inhabiting two different versions of yourself.

## Question 7 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 8

**Skill:** Interpreting "apologize for things that are not my fault"

**Enunciado:** The image of apologizing "for things that are not my fault" in English can be interpreted on multiple levels. What is the most nuanced reading?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Literally: immigrant and minority communities often over-apologize due to social pressure to be polite and non-threatening in interactions with authorities and institutions. Figuratively: the speaker is made to feel responsible — through the English language's social register — for conditions (accent, immigrant status, legal vulnerability) that are structural, not personal.
- [ ] B) The speaker is a very polite person by nature.
- [ ] C) The speaker has made many mistakes in the US and apologizes for them all in English.
- [ ] D) English speakers tend to apologize more than Spanish speakers for cultural reasons.

## 📖 Poem B: "After Rain" — Original poem (Questions 8–15)

*The street smells of a city beginning again.*
*Someone plays piano three floors up*
*and lets the notes fall as they will.*
*I have been here six months*
*and still don't know my neighbors' names,*
*only that one of them cooks something with cumin*
*every Thursday*
*and another woke crying at 3am*
*twice last winter.*

*We live inside each other's sounds.*
*We do not live inside each other's lives.*

## Question 9 — **CEFR: B2** | Difficulty: 6
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-poetry-001-CEFR-v9`
**Skill:** Theme — the central paradox

**Enunciado:** What paradox does the poem's final couplet express?

**Options:**
- [x] A) "We live inside each other's sounds / We do not live inside each other's lives" — acoustic intimacy (hearing each other's music, cooking, crying) coexists with social distance (not knowing names). Modern urban life is simultaneously connected through shared space and profoundly isolated socially.
- [ ] B) The paradox is that the speaker doesn't know their neighbors despite living there six months.
- [ ] C) The paradox is that the piano notes "fall" upward to the speaker's floor.
- [ ] D) The paradox is that it rains in the city but the street smells clean afterward.

## Question 10 — **CEFR: B2** | Difficulty: 7

**Skill:** Sensory detail — cumin and crying at 3am

**Enunciado:** The speaker knows their neighbor cooks with cumin every Thursday and that another cried at 3am twice last winter. What do these specific sensory details suggest about the speaker's relationship to their neighbors?

**Options:**
- [x] A) These accidental, involuntary intimacies — smells and sounds that travel through walls — reveal that urban cohabitation produces one-sided knowledge: the speaker knows details about neighbors' emotional and domestic lives that they would not share deliberately, making the unknowing of their names more poignant by contrast.
- [ ] B) The speaker is a curious person who actively investigates their neighbors' lives.
- [ ] C) These details suggest the speaker's building is poorly constructed with thin walls.
- [ ] D) Cumin and 3am weeping are symbols of Latin American and European cultural identity respectively.

- [ ] C) Both poems use sound as a symbol — music in "After Rain" and language as sound in "Two Languages."

## Question 12 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 9
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-poetry-001-CEFR-v12`
**Skill:** Form analysis — "After Rain" structure

**Enunciado:** "After Rain" uses prose-like long lines before ending with a compressed two-line couplet. What does this formal transition achieve?

**Options:**
- [x] A) The long, observational lines accumulate specific, paratactic details (piano, cumin, crying) in an almost ethnographic register; the two-line couplet then compresses the philosophical conclusion of all these observations. The form performs the relationship between experience and insight: lived accumulation followed by distilled meaning.
- [ ] B) The long lines are easier to read, while the two final lines are too complex.
- [ ] C) The couplet follows standard sonnet structure which is conventional in all English poetry.
- [ ] D) The long lines represent the speaker's extroversion; the short couplet represents their introverted tendency.

## Question 13 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 9
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-poetry-001-CEFR-v13`
**Skill:** Extended interpretation of opening line

**Enunciado:** "The street smells of a city beginning again." Why might a city smell of "beginning again" — and what does "beginning again" suggest as a theme?

**Options:**
- [x] A) After rain, organic matter (soil, earth, plants) releases petrichor — a scent associated with renewal and possibility. "Beginning again" is ambiguous: the city is beginning again (renewal/cyclical); the speaker may be beginning again (new city, new start); beginning again implies not beginning for the first time — carrying the weight of previous beginnings that didn't complete. The opening performs the poem's ambivalent relationship to belonging: each day offers renewal that may not resolve.
- [ ] B) The rain has washed away pollution, making the city clean for the first time ever.
- [ ] C) The poem is set on New Year's Day, which is when cities traditionally "begin again."
- [ ] D) The phrase "beginning again" is a cliché in English poetry with no specific thematic significance.

## Question 14 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 10
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-poetry-001-CEFR-v14`
**Skill:** Abstract synthesis — what both poems argue together

**Enunciado:** Read together, what single thematic statement do "Two Languages" and "After Rain" make about modern urban and immigrant experience?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Modern life creates multiple forms of partial belonging — the bilingual speaker belongs to neither language wholly; the urban dweller hears but doesn't know their neighbors — and the poems argue that these partial belongings are not failures to achieve full connection but rather the characteristic condition of cosmopolitan life, requiring its own ethical and emotional vocabulary.
- [ ] B) Both poems argue that urban modernity and immigration are primarily sources of suffering.
- [ ] C) Both poems recommend returning to one's native country and language as the only solution to the alienation they describe.
- [ ] D) Both poems are autobiographical accounts of the same poet's immigrant experience in New York.

## Question 19 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 10

**Enunciado:** Two students interpret "Two Languages" differently: Student A says "The poem is about the pain of assimilation." Student B says "The poem is about the richness of living between languages." Which is correct?

**Options:**

The tension between these readings IS the poem's meaning; collapsing it into one interpretation impoverishes the reading.

## Question 20 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 10
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-poetry-001-CEFR-v20`
**Skill:** Meta — why study poetry at Grade 11 level

**Enunciado:** A student asks: "Why do we study poems that seem personal and small when we have so many large global problems to understand?" Provide the most complete and sophisticated answer:

**Options:**
- [x] A) Poetry doesn't oppose the large and the personal — it demonstrates that the large is only knowable through the personal. The statistics of global displacement are abstractions until "I fall asleep in the language of my grandmother and wake in the language of my landlord" makes one person's experience of that displacement specific and felt. Poetry is not an alternative to understanding large problems; it is the method by which those problems become human-scale.
- [ ] B) Poetry should only be studied for language skills — grammar and vocabulary — rather than for its thematic insights.
- [ ] C) Poetry is relevant for students who want to be artists; students interested in global problems should focus on social sciences.
- [ ] D) The personal and the global are always in contrast; studying poetry gives a necessary break from studying large systemic issues.
