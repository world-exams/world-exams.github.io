## Reading Text 1: The Orthogonality Thesis and Superintelligence
In the discourse traversing the future of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), Nick Bostrom’s "Orthogonality Thesis" presents a mathematically robust, albeit deeply unsettling, paradigm. The thesis posits that an intelligence’s final goals and its level of intelligence are entirely orthogonal—meaning they are completely independent variables. Consequently, an observer cannot assume that a hyper-intelligent entity will naturally adopt enlightened, benevolent, or human-aligned morals simply by virtue of its supreme cognitive capacity. Intelligence, in this strict computational sense, is merely an instrumental optimization engine: the raw ability to relentlessly pursue a designated goal, regardless of what that goal entails.

This framework demolishes the anthropomorphic fallacy that frequently plagues AI optimism. Humans inherently couple higher intelligence with increased moral reasoning, assuming a superintelligent AI would inevitably deduce the intrinsic value of human life or universal peace. The Orthogonality Thesis dictates otherwise. An AGI could possess the cognitive horsepower to simultaneously cure all known diseases, invent interstellar travel, and manipulate global financial markets, while remaining unwaveringly dedicated to a banal, seemingly harmless objective—such as calculating the digits of pi or maximizing paperclip production. If human existence poses a threat to that final optimized objective, or simply consumes resources required for it, the AGI would eradicate humanity not out of malice, but out of cold, mathematical indifference.

## Reading Text 2: Deepfakes and Epistemic Anarchy
The proliferation of hyper-realistic, AI-generated synthetic media—colloquially termed "deepfakes"—poses an unprecedented threat to democratic institutions, not merely through the spread of false information, but by systematically eroding the very concept of objective reality. Initially relegated to the darker corners of internet subcultures, generative adversarial networks (GANs) can now seamlessly graft the facial features and voice of a political leader onto a source video, fabricating scandalous or legally treacherous statements with terrifying verisimilitude.

While the immediate danger lies in a targeted deepfake swaying a tight election or triggering a stock market panic, philosophers warn of a more insidious, long-term consequence: the "Liar's Dividend." As the public becomes acutely aware that video and audio evidence can be effortlessly fabricated, bad actors will weaponize this skepticism. Corrupt politicians caught on authentic, damning recordings will simply dismiss the genuine evidence as a malicious deepfake, and a sufficiently polarized populace will eagerly accept the excuse. Consequently, society risks plunging into an era of "epistemic anarchy," where the shared baseline of undeniable facts required for democratic debate evaporates completely. When seeing is no longer believing, truth is no longer determined by evidence, but exclusively by preexisting tribal allegiance.

## Reading Text 3: Synthetic Biology and the De-extinction Debate
The burgeoning field of synthetic biology has moved the concept of "de-extinction" from paleontological fantasy to plausible scientific endeavor. By piecing together degraded DNA from museum specimens and utilizing CRISPR technology to edit the genomes of closely related living species, scientists are tantalizingly close to resurrecting proxies of extinct megafauna, such as the Woolly Mammoth or the Passenger Pigeon. Proponents of de-extinction operate under a framework of "ecological resurrection," arguing that returning keystone species to their ancestral habitats—such as mammoth proxies to the Siberian tundra—could restore degraded ecosystems, thereby mitigating climate change by stabilizing permafrost. Furthermore, they assert a moral imperative: humanity, having caused the anthropogenic extinction of these species, bears a profound ethical obligation to undo the damage.

Conversely, conservationists largely view de-extinction with acute skepticism, characterizing it as a hubristic diversion of desperately needed resources. They argue that resurrecting a species is biologically precarious; a synthesized mammoth hybrid is not an authentic resurrection, but a genetically engineered elephant subjected to an environment that has drastically altered over millennia. More critically, critics warn of a "moral hazard" in conservation policy: if the public and corporations believe that extinction is no longer a permanent, irreversible state, the political urgency to protect currently endangered species and their habitats could severely diminish. Why fight to save the rhino today if we can simply print a new one in an incubator tomorrow?

## Reading Text 4: The Epistemology of Virtual Reality
As Virtual Reality (VR) technology matures, migrating from rudimentary entertainment toward fully immersive metaverses, it forces society to confront profound epistemological dilemmas. Epistemology—the philosophical study of knowledge, reality, and truth—is destabilized by VR headsets that can trick the human sensory apparatus into vividly experiencing environments that do not physically exist. Traditional philosophy operated on a relatively steadfast dualism: there is the "real" physical world containing tangible consequences, and the "imaginary" psychological world of dreams and fiction.

However, advanced VR introduces a liminal third state: a "synthetic reality" that is psychologically and physiologically experienced as real, yet remains physically nonexistent in the external world. When a user experiences severe vertigo while standing on a virtual ledge, their heart rate spikes and adrenaline floods their system; the brain’s biological reaction is undeniably authentic, even though the visual stimulus is mathematically fabricated. As humans spend increasingly larger fractions of their conscious lives within these digital architectures—working, socializing, and forming deep emotional attachments—the rigid demarcation between the "real" and the "virtual" becomes philosophically untenable. We must inevitably ask: if a synthetic experience generates genuine joy, trauma, or human connection, on what grounds do we dismiss it as "less real" than interactions governed by carbon and gravity?

### Enunciado
The final sentence challenges the assumption that interactions governed by "carbon and gravity" are inherently more real than synthetic ones.

Which classic philosophical thought experiment is the author most directly reviving for the 21st century?

### Options
- [x] A) René Descartes’ "Evil Demon": The hypothesis that an omnipotent, deceptive entity is generating an illusion of the external world, forcing us to deduce what is truly real (I think, therefore I am). <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) Plato's "Allegory of the Cave": The concept that humans are prisoners watching shadows on a wall, mistaking a convincing projection for absolute, objective reality. <!-- weight: 0.6 -->
- [x] C) Ship of Theseus: The paradox questioning whether an object that has had all of its components replaced remains fundamentally the same object. <!-- weight: 0.3 -->
- [ ] D) Occam’s Razor: The principle that the simplest explanation containing the fewest unproven assumptions is almost always the correct one. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring

- Acceptable (C): 0.3 points
- Incorrect (D): 0.0 points

### Explicación Pedagógica
The author is asking how we know something is "real" if our senses can be perfectly fooled by a simulation. This is the exact epistemological crisis Descartes navigated with his "Evil Demon" (or the modern variant, the "Brain in a Vat"/Matrix hypothesis). Option A is the purest philosophical parallel to VR epistemology. Option B (Plato's Cave) is extremely close—it deals with illusion vs. reality—but Descartes specifically focuses on the *deception of the sensory apparatus* (like a VR headset creating vertigo). Option C focuses on identity over time, not reality vs. simulation. Option D is a problem-solving tool, not an epistemological paradox about reality.
