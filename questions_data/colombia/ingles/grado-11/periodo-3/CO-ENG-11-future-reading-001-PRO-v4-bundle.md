## Reading Text 1: The Illusion of the "Useless Class"
In debates surrounding the rapid automation of the workforce by Artificial Intelligence, a chilling dystopian narrative has emerged: the imminent creation of a "useless class." Proponents of this view, often championed by Silicon Valley technocrats, argue that AI will not simply replace manual labor, but will eclipse human cognitive abilities, rendering vast swathes of the population economically superfluous. To ameliorate this impending disaster, they advocate for Universal Basic Income (UBI)—a monthly government stipend designed to keep the displaced masses docile and fed.

However, this argument is profoundly flawed, functioning less as a genuine socioeconomic solution and more as a preemptive absolution for the tech industry's monopolistic practices. The "useless class" narrative fundamentally misunderstands the nature of human labor. Work is not merely the mechanical execution of tasks to produce capital; it is a primary vector for social cohesion, identity, and psychological purpose. By proposing UBI as a panacea, tech billionaires are essentially attempting to bribe society into accepting a future where a microscopic elite controls all modes of production and cultural output. Instead of surrendering to inevitable obsolescence funded by UBI, society must fiercely protect the dignity of labor, aggressively taxing automation to fund massive public works and human-centric sectors—like education and elder care—where empathy makes the human touch irreplaceable.

## Reading Text 2: The Egotism of the Martian Dream
In recent years, the colonization of Mars has shifted from the realm of speculative fiction to the concrete mission statements of heavily funded aerospace corporations. Figures like Elon Musk tout interplanetary settlement as a necessary "backup drive" for humanity—an essential insurance policy against existential threats such as asteroid impacts, nuclear war, or catastrophic climate change. The narrative is undeniably intoxicating, wrapping the pioneering spirit of human exploration in the seemingly noble goal of preserving the species.

However, stripped of its romanticized veneer, the Martian dream reveals a profoundly dangerous ideological escapism. The assertion that we require a "backup planet" operates on the tacit, fatalistic assumption that Earth is a lost cause. Trillions of dollars and brilliant engineering intellects are being funneled into designing pressurized habitats for an irradiated, freezing wasteland, while millions suffer from a lack of clean water and sustainable infrastructure here at home. Furthermore, the very mindset that catalyzed our ecological crisis on Earth—the belief that the environment is merely a passive resource to be endlessly extracted and conquered—is exactly what drives the ambition to "terraform" Mars. Exploring space to gain scientific knowledge is a worthy endeavor; colonizing it because we refuse to clean up our own planetary mess is an act of intergalactic hubris.

## Reading Text 3: Digital Immortality and the Loss of Meaning
The transhumanist movement eagerly anticipates the advent of "mind uploading"—the theoretical process of mapping the human brain's neural network and transferring consciousness into a digital substrate. To its advocates, digital immortality represents the ultimate escape from the frailties of biology. Unshackled from disease and cellular degradation, a digitized consciousness could theoretically exist indefinitely within simulated realities, accumulating infinite knowledge and experiencing limitless virtual pleasures. Death, in this view, is simply a technological bug waiting to be patched.

This techno-utopian vision, aside from its staggering technological hurdles, relies on a fundamentally impoverished philosophy of what constitutes human significance. Scarcity generates value. It is precisely the finite, fragile nature of biological existence that imbues our choices, our relationships, and our time with meaning. If time is limitless, urgency vanishes; if physical risk is eliminated, courage becomes obsolete. In a digital eternity where every mistake can be undone with a "reset" command, human experiences would flatline into a state of perpetual, apathetic hedonism. Furthermore, to believe that a digitally duplicated pattern of synapses is genuinely *you*—rather than merely a sophisticated software clone functioning after your actual biological death—requires a leap of faith identical to the most orthodox religious doctrines, masked entirely in the vocabulary of silicon.

## Reading Text 4: The Algorithm of Empathy
The integration of specialized AI in psychotherapy has provoked intense backlash from the medical establishment. Chatbots utilizing Large Language Models have been deployed to offer cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) to individuals suffering from depression and anxiety. Proponents argue that AI provides an infinitely scalable, low-cost solution to a global mental health crisis characterized by an alarming shortage of human therapists. They note that patients often feel more comfortable disclosing shameful secrets to an algorithm, knowing they are entirely free from the agonizing threat of human judgment.

Critics counter that delegating psychiatric care to servers is a dangerous simulacrum of medicine. Therapy requires profound empathetic attunement—the ability to read subtle micro-expressions, interpret silences, and genuinely share in human suffering. An AI merely processes textual input through a statistical probability matrix to output the most expected, reassuring response; it feels nothing. However, this opposition often ignores a disturbing reality: millions of people currently receive absolutely no care. The debate incorrectly assumes a binary choice between a highly trained human psychiatrist and an AI chatbot. In the global reality of crippling poverty and resource scarcity, the actual choice is most frequently between an AI chatbot and agonizing isolation. Dismissing algorithmic therapy out of deference to an ideal standard of human empathy is an elitist standpoint that effectively abandons the most vulnerable.
