## Reading Text 1: The Kessler Syndrome
Since the launch of Sputnik in 1957, humanity has treated Low Earth Orbit (LEO) as an infinite expanse, treating the cosmos with the same reckless disregard historically shown to the oceans. Today, LEO is dangerously congested with orbital debris—defunct satellites, spent rocket stages, and millions of microscopic paint flecks traveling at speeds exceeding 17,500 miles per hour. At such velocities, even a fragment the size of a screw possesses the kinetic energy of an exploding hand grenade, capable of instantly disabling a multi-billion-dollar communications satellite or fatally compromising the International Space Station.

In 1978, NASA scientist Donald Kessler proposed a chilling theoretical scenario that now bears his name: the Kessler Syndrome. Kessler modeled a catastrophic cascade effect wherein the density of orbital garbage reaches a critical threshold. A single collision generates a cloud of shrapnel, which in turn strikes other satellites, generating exponentially more debris. This runaway chain reaction would act as an impenetrable, high-velocity blockade, rendering LEO entirely unusable for generations. If the Kessler Syndrome is triggered, humanity risks confining itself permanently to a terrestrial prison, abruptly ending the era of global GPS, satellite internet, and deep-space exploration.

## Reading Text 2: The Internet of Bodies (IoB)
We have normalized the "Internet of Things" (IoT)—a network of smart refrigerators, thermostats, and cars continuously transmitting data. The logical, yet profoundly invasive, successor to this paradigm is the "Internet of Bodies" (IoB). The IoB refers to a network of human bodies interconnected through implanted, ingested, or highly advanced wearable micro-technologies. These devices do not merely count footsteps; they continuously monitor deeply intimate biometric data—heart rate variability, glucose fluctuations, and even neurological firing patterns—transmitting this telemetry to centralized corporate servers in real-time.

While the medical sector hails the IoB as a revolution in preventative healthcare—capable of predicting a cardiac event days before the patient feels any symptoms—privacy advocates are sounding the alarm. Telemetry from the human body is the ultimate, non-redactable dataset. If a corporation algorithmically analyzes a user's biological stress responses to specific advertisements or political speeches, it achieves an unprecedented capacity for psychological manipulation. Furthermore, the legal frameworks governing data ownership are woefully unequipped for the IoB era. If an implanted pacemaker generates a diagnostic dataset, does that data belong to the patient whose heart pumped the blood, or the tech conglomerate that manufactured the silicon?

## Reading Text 3: The Technological Singularity
Famed futurist Ray Kurzweil popularized the concept of the "Technological Singularity"—a hypothetical point in the future when artificial intelligence surpasses human intelligence, leading to a runaway cycle of self-improvement. Because an AI could design an even smarter AI faster than human engineers, technological progress would become asymptotic, developing at an incomprehensibly rapid pace. According to Kurzweil, this event horizon will not eradicate humanity. Rather, he envisions a biological-computational merger, where nanobots seamlessly integrate with our neocortex, exponentially expanding human cognitive bandwidth and effectively turning the species into immortal cyborgs.

Critics from the humanities, however, view this techno-rapture with profound apprehension, attacking the subtext of the Singularity thesis. The assumption that uploading the human mind to a cloud server equates to "immortality" relies on a severely reductive view of consciousness. It presumes the mind is merely a complex software algorithm running on 'wetware' (the brain), discounting the inextricably embodied nature of human emotion. Hormones, gut microbiomes, and visceral physical sensations are not peripheral accessories to the human experience; they are the core architecture of emotion. A purely digital, disembodied mind, stripped of its biological endocrine system, might possess infinite computational power, but it would be utterly devoid of the capacity to feel love, fear, or awe.

The critics argue that "visceral physical sensations are not peripheral accessories to the human experience; they are the core architecture of emotion."

Which major psychological/neurological theory directly supports the critics' argument against disembodied AI?

### Options
- [x] A) The Somatic Marker Hypothesis (Damasio): The theory that emotional processes and physical, bodily sensations (like a racing heart or a "gut feeling") are strictly necessary to guide rational decision-making and consciousness. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) Embodied Cognition: The theory that many features of cognition, whether human or otherwise, are deeply dependent upon the physical characteristics of the physical body of an agent. <!-- weight: 0.6 -->
- [x] C) Dualism (Descartes): The theory that the mind and the physical body are two completely separate substances that can exist independently. <!-- weight: 0.0 --> (This supports the AI proponents)
