## Question 1 — **CEFR: B1+** | Difficulty: 5
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-narrative-002-CEFR-v1`
**Skill:** Literary term — metaphor vs. simile

**Enunciado:** Identify whether each is a metaphor or simile: "Life is a journey" / "Life is like a journey"

**Options:**
- [x] A) "Life is a journey" = metaphor (direct comparison); "Life is like a journey" = simile (uses 'like' for comparison).
- [ ] B) Both are metaphors because both compare life to a journey.
- [ ] C) Both are similes because both make comparisons.
- [ ] D) "Life is a journey" is a simile; "Life is like a journey" is a metaphor.

**Skill:** Allusion — identifying reference and function

**Enunciado:** A story ends: "Like Sisyphus, he returned to work each Monday without hope." This ending uses:

**Options:**
- [x] A) Classical allusion — reference to the Greek myth of Sisyphus (condemned to eternally roll a boulder uphill) to convey the protagonist's sense of futile, repetitive, hopeless labor without having to describe it directly.
- [ ] B) Extended metaphor — comparing a character to a stone throughout the narrative.
- [ ] C) Personification — giving human qualities to the concept of work.
- [ ] D) Foreshadowing — predicting that the character will eventually give up and stop working.

## Question 7 — **CEFR: B2** | Difficulty: 7
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-narrative-002-CEFR-v7`
**Skill:** Tone analysis — using diction to identify tone

**Enunciado:** A narrator describes a character: "She glided into the room, her presence preceding her like a warm breeze." What tone does this diction establish?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Admiring and warm — "glided" (graceful, effortless movement), "warm breeze" (pleasant, non-threatening), "presence preceding her" (commanding positive attention) all construct a portrait of admirable, appealing authority.
- [ ] B) Ominous — "preceding her" suggests something threatening about to happen.
- [ ] C) Comic — "glided" is an exaggerated, humorous verb for describing someone walking.
- [ ] D) Formal — the vocabulary is too advanced for the passage to have any emotional tone.

## Question 8 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 8
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-narrative-002-CEFR-v8`
**Skill:** Dramatic irony — reader knows vs. character knows

**Enunciado:** In a story, the reader has been shown that a character's best friend is secretly betraying him. The character then says: "I don't know what I would do without your support." This creates:

**Options:**
- [x] A) Dramatic irony — the gap between the character's trust and the reader's knowledge of the betrayal creates painful emotional tension; the sincere statement becomes tragically ironic because the reader understands its double meaning.
- [ ] B) Verbal irony — the character says the opposite of what they mean.
- [ ] C) Situational irony — events turn out differently than the character expected.
- [ ] D) Foreshadowing — the statement predicts that the character will eventually be betrayed.

## Section 3: Advanced Literary Language

## Question 16 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 8
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-narrative-002-CEFR-v16`
**Skill:** Extended metaphor vs. isolated metaphor

**Enunciado:** An author writes: "The corporation was a machine. Its employees were gears. Its managers were engineers who oiled and adjusted them. When gears broke, they were replaced." This is:

**Options:**
- [x] A) An extended metaphor — the machine metaphor is sustained and developed through multiple vehicles (gears, engineers, oiling) rather than appearing once and being abandoned.
- [ ] B) A simile — the author compares the corporation to a machine using "as" or "like."
- [ ] C) A mixed metaphor — the author inconsistently combines multiple unrelated metaphors.
- [ ] D) Personification — giving the corporation human characteristics.

**Enunciado:** Story A says early on: "She always kept the back door unlocked — a habit that would one day cost her dearly." Story B shows the reader a character preparing a surprise for a friend who doesn't know about it. Which technique is which?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Story A = foreshadowing (authorial hint about future events). Story B = dramatic irony (reader knows something a character doesn't — the character expects a normal day, the reader expects a surprise).
- [ ] B) Both are examples of dramatic irony.
- [ ] C) Story A = dramatic irony; Story B = foreshadowing.
- [ ] D) Neither story uses a recognizable literary device; both are just narrative information.

## Question 18 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 9
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-narrative-002-CEFR-v18`
**Skill:** Ambiguity in literary language — resisting single readings

**Enunciado:** Elena "laughed and then cried, and then sat on the floor in her pajamas for a long time, neither one nor the other." A student says this is ambiguous. What is the LITERARY VALUE of this ambiguity?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Ambiguity resists the reductive logic of a single interpretation — it creates a space where multiple, contradictory emotional states exist simultaneously, mirroring the real experience of complex feeling; forcing the text to resolve ambiguity would betray the psychological truth it tries to capture.
- [ ] B) The author should have been clearer — literary ambiguity is a failure of communication.
- [ ] C) Ambiguity is only valuable in poetry; in prose, clarity is always preferable.
- [ ] D) The ambiguity suggests the author was unable to decide how Elena truly felt.

## Question 19 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 10
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-narrative-002-CEFR-v19`
**Skill:** C1 — integrating literary analysis with linguistic analysis

**Enunciado:** The phrase "the kind of thing old women said when they were afraid of being forgotten" is narrated through Elena's past perspective. How does the story use this initial narrator's voice strategically?

**Options:**
- [x] A) By presenting Elena's dismissive past judgment as the opening framing, the story sets up a corrective arc — the reader begins with Elena's early dismissal and ends understanding the grandmother was correct. The initial narrator's voice performs its own unreliability; we share Elena's limited understanding at first, then come to see past it.
- [ ] B) The initial narrator's voice is irrelevant — what matters is the final scene, not the opening.
- [ ] C) The phrase demonstrates that Elena had a difficult relationship with her grandmother and never truly valued her presence.
- [ ] D) Old women in Latin American fiction always represent wisdom; the dismissal is therefore immediately ironic to any informed reader.

## Question 20 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 10
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-narrative-002-CEFR-v20`
**Skill:** Critical evaluation — the purpose of studying literary devices

**Enunciado:** A student complains: "Why do we need to name literary devices? The story affects me emotionally whether I know what a 'simile' is or not." Provide the most sophisticated counter-argument:

**Options:**
- [x] A) Naming literary devices creates a metalanguage that allows us to move from passive appreciation (feeling) to active analysis (understanding HOW the text produces its effects) — giving us tools to understand, evaluate, and compare texts more precisely, to see how specific choices create specific effects, and to apply those techniques consciously in our own writing.
- [ ] B) Naming literary devices is required for passing exams — that is the only practical justification.
- [ ] C) The student is correct — emotional response is always superior to analytical understanding.
- [ ] D) Naming devices is necessary because otherwise stories from different cultures cannot be compared.
