## Master Context B: The Mechanics of Soft Power (Questions 11-20)

In international relations, the concept of "soft power," coined (11) _______ political scientist Joseph Nye in the late 1980s, refers to the ability of a country to persuade others to do what it wants without resorting to force or coercion. While "hard power" relies on military strength and economic sanctions, soft power rests (12) _______ three pillars: a country's culture, its political values, and its foreign policies.

(13) _______ a nation's culture is universally appealing, its capacity to influence global norms increases exponentially. For instance, the global popularity of American cinema, South Korean music, and European intellectual traditions (14) _______ given these regions significant diplomatic leverage. When citizens of other nations admire a country's ideals, their governments are (15) _______ less likely to adopt antagonistic postures towards it.

However, soft power is fragile. It takes decades to build (16) _______ can be lost almost overnight if a nation's foreign policy is perceived as hypocritical or unjust. Moreover, in the age of rapid digital disinformation, state-sponsored propaganda often attempts to (17) _______ the authentic soft power of rivals. Therefore, to (18) _______ effectively in the 21st century, foreign ministries must prioritize cultural diplomacy and genuinely uphold the democratic values they (19) _______ to champion globally. Without authenticity, soft power turns (20) _______ an empty facade.
