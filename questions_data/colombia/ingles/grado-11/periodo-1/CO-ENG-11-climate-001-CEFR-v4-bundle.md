## 📖 Master Context A: The Science-Policy Gap (Questions 1–10)

Every major international scientific body agrees: human activity is warming the planet at an unprecedented rate, and the consequences — rising seas, extreme weather, ecosystem collapse — are already being observed. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has repeatedly warned that without immediate, large-scale emissions reductions, the most severe impacts become unavoidable.

Yet the political response has been, at best, fragmented. Nations continue to negotiate, pledge, and revise targets while global emissions continue to rise. The 2015 Paris Agreement represented a historic diplomatic achievement — 196 countries agreeing to limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels — but the agreement was built on voluntary, nationally determined contributions with no binding enforcement mechanism. Even if every nation met its current pledges, models suggest the world is still on track for approximately 2.7°C of warming by the century's end.

Analysts identify several structural barriers. First, the **asymmetry of timescales**: politicians operate on four-year electoral cycles, while climate change unfolds over decades and centuries, making ambitious climate action politically costly in the short term. Second, the **concentration of economic interests**: the fossil fuel industry represents trillions of dollars in existing assets and employs millions of people globally. Transitioning away from these industries requires managing powerful resistance. Third, a persistent **information asymmetry** exists between climate scientists and the general public, which disinformation campaigns actively exploit.

The growing consensus among scholars is that effective climate policy requires not just technological solutions, but a fundamental transformation of political economy — restructuring incentives so that long-term planetary survival aligns with short-term electoral and business interests.

## 📖 Master Context B: Youth Climate Movements & Intergenerational Justice (Questions 11–20)

In September 2019, an estimated four million people participated in the Global Climate Strike, a movement largely organized by and for young people. The symbolic figurehead was Greta Thunberg, a Swedish teenager who had begun striking outside her nation's parliament every Friday in August 2018. Her "Fridays for Future" movement spread to over 150 countries within a year, representing a remarkable mobilization of youth political agency.

The movement's core argument invokes the concept of **intergenerational justice**: the idea that decisions made by current generations impose costs on future generations who had no voice in making them. Young climate activists argue, with some legal and philosophical force, that being born into a world with irreversible climate damage — consequences they did not cause — constitutes a profound violation of their rights. Several youth-led legal cases have advanced this argument in international courts, with varying degrees of success.

Critics of youth-led climate movements sometimes argue that they prioritize symbolism over substance, and that effective change only comes through engaging with the formal mechanisms of governance — writing legislation, lobbying, voting. Supporters counter that the movements have achieved something essential: they have shifted the **Overton window** of climate politics, making previously radical proposals (such as a complete ban on new fossil fuel infrastructure) legitimate subjects of mainstream political debate.

Whether youth activism alone can drive sufficient policy change remains an open question. What is clear is that this generation represents the first cohort in human history to have been raised with a scientific consensus on human-caused climate change as background knowledge — and they are refusing to treat it as an abstraction.
