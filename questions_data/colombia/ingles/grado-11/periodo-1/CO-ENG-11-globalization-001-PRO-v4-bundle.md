## Master Context: The Political Trilemma of the World Economy (Questions 1-20)

For decades following the Cold War, the prevailing consensus among the world's elite policy-makers was that deep economic globalization was an unmitigated good. The reduction of trade barriers, the free flow of international capital, and the integration of global supply chains were expected to lift all boats, promoting democracy and unprecedented wealth. However, as inequalities deepened and populist backlashes erupted in established democracies, economists were forced to reassess this neoliberal paradigm.

Harvard economist Dani Rodrik crystalized this reassessment through what he termed the "Globalization Paradox," or the "Political Trilemma of the World Economy." Rodrik posits that a society cannot simultaneously achieve three things: hyper-globalization (deep economic integration), national sovereignty (the right of a nation state to write its own rules), and mass democracy (domestic politics responsive to the will of the people). According to the trilemma, you can only have two of these three.

If a nation desires hyper-globalization and national sovereignty, it must sacrifice mass democracy. In this scenario, the "Golden Straitjacket," domestic policies must be rigorously aligned with the demands of global markets and international investors—requiring low taxes, deregulation, and tight monetary policy—regardless of what the domestic electorate actually votes for. If a nation strays from market demands to satisfy its voters, capital will flee, leading to economic collapse. Thus, political choices are heavily constrained, rendering democratic elections largely hollow.

Conversely, if a society wants to maintain deep democracy and hyper-globalization, it must sacrifice the nation-state. This implies a system of global federalism, where democratic politics are elevated to a global level (like a massively expanded European Union), creating global regulatory bodies to manage global markets. But this requires nations to surrender their sovereignty.

Finally, if a society demands both national sovereignty and a meaningful domestic democracy (where voters can choose regulations, labor laws, and tax rates that differ from global capital demands), it must restrict the degree of its globalization. This is the "Bretton Woods compromise," a system where international trade is encouraged, but capital flows are moderated to allow nations the policy space to address domestic social contracts. Rodrik's stark warning is that pushing for unchecked hyper-globalization invariably exacts a high political toll, a reality that the political establishments of the early 21st century largely ignored at their peril.
