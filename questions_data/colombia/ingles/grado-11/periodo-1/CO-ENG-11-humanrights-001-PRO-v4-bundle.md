## Master Context A: The Universal Declaration (Questions 1-10)

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) is a milestone document in the history of human rights. Drafted by representatives from different legal and cultural backgrounds from all regions of the world, it was proclaimed by the United Nations General Assembly in Paris on December 10, 1948. For the first time, it set out fundamental human rights to be universally protected.

However, the UDHR is a declaration, not a treaty. This means it is not legally binding on its own. Instead, it serves as the foundational text that inspired over 80 international human rights treaties, two of the most important being the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR). Together with the UDHR, these two covenants form what is known as the International Bill of Human Rights.

A major challenge in international law is enforcement. When a state violates these rights, the primary mechanism for accountability rests within that state's own domestic courts. If domestic remedies are exhausted or fail, individuals may sometimes appeal to regional courts, such as the Inter-American Court of Human Rights, based in Costa Rica. Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch are prominent NGOs that investigate and publicize abuses, "naming and shaming" violators to generate international pressure, though they have no legal authority to punish crimes.

## Master Context B: Digital Rights as Human Rights (Questions 11-20)

As the world becomes deeply embedded in digital infrastructure, a new frontier of human rights has emerged: digital rights. The United Nations has repeatedly affirmed that "the same rights that people have offline must also be protected online." This particularly applies to the freedom of expression and the right to privacy, as outlined in Articles 19 and 12 of the UDHR, respectively.

One of the most pressing contemporary issues is internet shutdowns. Governments in various regions have deliberately restricted internet access during elections, protests, or civil unrest to control the flow of information. Human rights advocates argue that these blackouts violate international law because they prevent citizens from accessing essential services, communicating with family, and exercising democratic rights. In 2016, the UN Human Rights Council passed a non-binding resolution unequivocally condemning measures that intentionally prevent or disrupt access to information online.

Another critical issue is mass surveillance. While states argue that gathering bulk digital data is necessary for national security and counter-terrorism, privacy advocates counter that unchecked surveillance creates a "chilling effect" on free speech. When people believe they are constantly watched, they self-censor, thereby undermining democratic participation. The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), implemented in 2018, is currently the most stringent legal framework defining how personal digital data must be handled, setting a global precedent for digital privacy rights.
