## 📖 Master Context A: The Wealth Gap and Its Invisible Architecture (Questions 1–10)

In 2023, Oxfam reported that the wealthiest 1% of humanity had accumulated more new wealth in the previous two years than the remaining 99% combined. This statistic captures the accelerating concentration of wealth that has characterized the global economy for decades. Yet economists and sociologists warn that raw numbers, while striking, miss the more complex mechanisms that perpetuate inequality across generations.

One of the most powerful of these mechanisms is what sociologists call **social reproduction**: the process by which social hierarchies are maintained and transmitted through institutions rather than individual merit. Children born into wealthy families benefit not only from financial inheritance but from access to superior education, professional networks, health care, and geographic mobility. These advantages compound over time, creating a structural environment in which social mobility — the ability to improve one's position in life through effort — becomes increasingly restricted.

The economist Raj Chetty's research in the United States found that a child born in the bottom income quintile has only an 8% chance of reaching the top quintile by adulthood, regardless of measured ability or effort. This challenges the meritocratic narrative — the belief that in a fair society, talent and hard work are sufficient to overcome starting disadvantage.

Critically, these inequalities are not confined to income. They manifest in **life expectancy**, **incarceration rates**, **access to justice**, and political representation. Research suggests that in countries with extreme wealth concentration, political systems shift to reflect the preferences of the wealthy rather than the majority — a phenomenon political scientists call **oligarchic drift**.

## 📖 Master Context B: Human Rights in the 21st Century — Enforcement Gaps (Questions 11–20)

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR), adopted by the United Nations in 1948, represents humanity's most ambitious collective statement of moral aspiration. Its 30 articles — covering civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights — were intended to serve as a universal standard that transcended national sovereignty. Yet 75 years after its adoption, the gap between the UDHR's aspirations and the lived reality of billions of people remains vast.

The core challenge is one of **enforcement**. The UDHR is not a legally binding treaty; it is a declaration, meaning that no international body has the authority to compel nations to comply. Its actual legal force depends largely on its incorporation into binding treaties — such as the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) — or into domestic constitutions. Even where such treaties exist, enforcement mechanisms are weak; a state found in violation can be condemned but rarely sanctioned.

A secondary challenge involves the relationship between **universality and cultural relativism**. Proponents of cultural relativism argue that some human rights norms — particularly those related to gender equality, LGBTQ+ rights, and freedom of religion — reflect Western liberal values rather than universal human values, and that their imposition violates the sovereignty of non-Western cultures. Human rights scholars counter that rights protecting human dignity from torture, arbitrary imprisonment, and starvation cannot be relativized without rendering the concept of human rights meaningless.

The 21st century has added new dimensions: digital rights (privacy in the age of mass surveillance), environmental rights (the right to a healthy environment), and the emerging debate over intergenerational rights. These expansions challenge international law to adapt frameworks designed for a 20th-century world.
