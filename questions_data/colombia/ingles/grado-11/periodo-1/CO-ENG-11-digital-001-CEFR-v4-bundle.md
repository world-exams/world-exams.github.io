## 📖 Master Context A: The Algorithm and the Public Sphere (Questions 1–10)

In 1962, philosopher Jürgen Habermas theorized the "public sphere" as an idealized space where citizens engage in rational, open debate free from the distortions of money or power. This concept predated the internet by decades. Yet digital media theorists now argue that social media platforms represent either the greatest expansion or the most catastrophic corruption of the public sphere in history — depending entirely on whom you ask.

The optimistic view holds that the internet has democratized information, allowing anyone with a connection to publish, debate, and access perspectives that traditional gatekeepers (newspapers, broadcasters, governments) once controlled. In theory, a teenager in Bogotá can now engage in real-time conversation with a researcher in Berlin or access the same scientific papers as a university professor.

The pessimistic view, increasingly supported by empirical research, argues that social media algorithms — designed to maximize **engagement** (time on platform) rather than **truth** — systematically amplify emotionally provocative and politically polarizing content. This creates what researchers call **filter bubbles**: personalized digital environments in which users are predominantly exposed to information that confirms their existing beliefs, while contradictory evidence is algorithmically suppressed.

The consequences extend beyond individual epistemic harm. Research from institutions including MIT found that false information spreads approximately six times faster on Twitter than true information. A study from Harvard's Shorenstein Center found that algorithmic amplification of polarizing content has measurably contributed to the erosion of shared factual baselines between political groups — a phenomenon that makes democratic deliberation increasingly difficult.

## 📖 Master Context B: Media Literacy as a 21st-Century Skill (Questions 11–20)

The term **media literacy** has evolved significantly since it was first used to describe the ability to critically analyze television and newspaper content in the 1970s. In the contemporary digital ecosystem, UNESCO defines media literacy as the set of competencies that enable citizens to "access, analyze, evaluate, and create media in various forms." This expanded definition reflects the recognition that in an information-saturated world, the ability to navigate, assess, and ethically produce media content is as fundamental as the ability to read and write.

However, the very nature of today's media environment creates profound challenges for this educational task. Traditional fact-checking — comparing a claim against established evidence — is insufficient when the problem is not merely isolated misinformation but a systemic **information disorder** characterized by three overlapping phenomena: **misinformation** (false content shared without malicious intent), **disinformation** (deliberately created and spread false content), and **malinformation** (true content used to cause harm, such as non-consensual sharing of private images).

The implication is that media literacy education must go beyond teaching students to identify "fake news" as if it were a discrete category. It must develop what media scholar Sonia Livingstone calls **critical data literacy** — the ability to understand how data systems, recommendation algorithms, and platform business models shape what information reaches us and why, before an individual even makes a conscious choice to believe or disbelieve something.
