## 📖 Master Context A: The Mental Health Crisis — Scale and Stigma (Questions 1–10)

The World Health Organization estimates that approximately one billion people globally live with a mental health condition — roughly one in eight humans. Depression and anxiety disorders alone account for nearly 280 million cases worldwide, making them among the most prevalent diseases in human history. Yet for decades, mental health received a fraction of the funding, research attention, and public health infrastructure afforded to physical conditions of comparable prevalence.

The disparity is partly explained by **stigma**: the complex web of negative attitudes, stereotypes, and social exclusion attached to mental illness. In many cultures, mental health conditions are still conflated with moral failure, personal weakness, or spiritual shortcoming. This cultural framing creates powerful disincentives to seek help, as disclosure can trigger discrimination in employment, relationships, and social standing. Research consistently finds that only between 20-30% of those with mental health conditions in low- and middle-income countries receive adequate treatment.

A second factor is the **infrastructure gap**. The WHO reports that most low-income countries spend less than 1% of their health budget on mental health. In contrast, physical health has millennia of established medical traditions, visible anatomical frameworks, and measurable biological markers that have made it easier to attract research investment and clinical infrastructure. Mental health's relative invisibility — symptoms are behavioral and self-reported, not typically visible through scans or blood tests — has historically made it easier to dismiss.

The consequences are severe: untreated mental health conditions cost the global economy an estimated $1 trillion per year in lost productivity. More critically, suicide — a largely preventable outcome of untreated depression — claims approximately 800,000 lives annually, exceeding deaths from many physical diseases that receive vastly more public health attention.

## 📖 Master Context B: Digital Technology and the Wellbeing Paradox (Questions 11–20)

In 2023, the US Surgeon General Vivek Murthy issued an unprecedented advisory warning that social media presents a "profound risk" to the mental health of adolescents. The advisory cited research linking heavy social media use with significantly elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and body image disorders, particularly among teenage girls. At the same time, digital technologies have enabled revolutionary mental health interventions: teletherapy, AI-powered mood tracking, online peer support communities, and crisis text lines that have reached millions who would never have sought traditional in-person help.

This is what researchers call the **wellbeing paradox** of digital technology: the same devices and platforms that are demonstrably harming the mental health of some populations are simultaneously enabling mental health care to reach others who were previously inaccessible to any form of support. The question is not, therefore, whether technology is "good" or "bad" for mental health, but rather which specific uses, by which populations, under which conditions, produce which outcomes.

This level of specificity is precisely what has been lacking in both public discourse and regulatory frameworks. Broad bans on social media for minors — proposed in multiple countries — risk eliminating the genuine benefits (community connection for isolated adolescents, mental health information access, creative expression) alongside the harms. Conversely, treating all social media as benign ignores robust evidence of algorithmic harm. Nuanced, evidence-based policy — targeting specific harmful mechanisms rather than technologies wholesale — represents the most defensible approach, though it is considerably more difficult to communicate and implement.
