## Master Context A: The Supply Chain Paradox (Questions 1-10)

For decades, the dominant philosophy in global manufacturing was "just-in-time" (JIT) production. Pioneered by Japanese automakers in the 1970s, JIT aims to increase efficiency and decrease waste by receiving goods only as they are needed in the production process, thereby reducing inventory costs. A car manufacturer, for instance, wouldn't keep thousands of windshields in a warehouse; instead, the windshields would arrive exactly on the day they were to be installed.

This system relies entirely on a predictable, frictionless global supply chain. When it works, it is a marvel of modern logistics, allowing companies to offer cheaper products to consumers. However, recent global crises have exposed a critical vulnerability: JIT production has completely eliminated the buffers that historically protected economies from sudden shocks.

When a factory in Asia shuts down temporarily, or a cargo ship blocks a major canal, the effects ripple across the globe almost instantly. Without stockpiled inventory, manufacturers on the other side of the world are forced to halt production within days. Consequently, economists are now debating whether the pursuit of maximum efficiency has sacrificed essential resilience. Some are advocating for a shift toward "just-in-case" strategies, which prioritize maintaining safety stocks over minimizing warehousing costs, even if it leads to higher consumer prices.

## Master Context B: The Gig Economy and the Future of Labor (Questions 11-20)

The rise of the "gig economy" has profoundly altered the traditional relationship between labor and capital. Rather than hiring full-time employees, companies increasingly rely on independent contractors, freelancers, and temporary workers who are paid per "gig" (task or project). This model, popularized by ride-sharing apps and freelance platforms, is touted by its proponents as the ultimate democratization of work. They argue it offers unparalleled flexibility, allowing individuals to choose their own hours and act as their own boss.

Critics, however, view the gig economy through a much harsher lens. They argue that this system represents a systemic transfer of risk from the corporation to the worker. A traditional employee benefits from a safety net: guaranteed minimum wages, health insurance, paid sick leave, and unemployment protection. Gig workers, classified legally as independent contractors, receive none of these protections. If demand drops, or if they fall ill, they absorb the financial shock entirely on their own.

Furthermore, economists warn of the long-term macroeconomic implications. A workforce lacking financial stability and benefits is less likely to buy homes, invest in retirement, or sustain consistent consumer spending. Therefore, the debate over how to legally classify and protect gig workers is not merely a dispute over labor rights; it is a fundamental question about what kind of social contract a modern economy can sustain.
