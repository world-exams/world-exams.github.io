## Text 3: The Mechanics of the Symphony
The classical symphony, as perfected by composers like Haydn and Mozart, is a marvel of structural engineering. It typically consists of four distinct movements, each adhering to a highly codified set of formal expectations. The first movement is almost invariably written in sonata-allegro form, a complex structural framework that functions as a musical narrative. It begins with the *exposition*, where two contrasting musical themes are introduced—the first often bold and rhythmic, the second more lyrical and subdued. This is followed by the *development* section, where the composer fragments, modulates, and intensely (11) ______ the original themes, creating a sense of dramatic instability and harmonic tension.

Finally, the movement resolves with the *recapitulation*, where the original themes return in their entirety, but this time harmonically aligned in the home key, providing a satisfying sense of logical completion. To the untrained listener, a Beethoven symphony might sound like a spontaneous outpouring of raw, unbridled emotion. However, musicologists understand that this profound emotional impact is actually generated through rigorous, almost mathematical adherence to formal structure. The genius of the classical composer lies not in abandoning the rules, but in exploiting the listener's subconscious (12) ______ of the sonata form to create moments of shocking surprise or overwhelming release. Therefore, listening to a symphony is not a passive sensory bath, but an active, intellectual (13) ______ in pattern recognition.

It was trusted implicitly in courtrooms, journalism, and scientific documentation as absolute, undeniable truth.

However, cultural critics like Susan Sontag systematically dismantled this illusion of objectivity. A photograph is never truly objective because the act of taking a picture inherently involves a series of highly subjective (17) ______. The photographer must choose where to stand, what to include in the frame, and more importantly, what to exclude. By cropping out the edges of a scene, managing the exposure, or waiting for a specific split-second of emotion, the photographer imposes their own specific narrative onto the world. Every photograph is a framing of reality, not reality itself. Today, in the era of sophisticated digital manipulation and AI-generated imagery, the original belief in photographic truth seems almost dangerously (18) ______. Yet, remarkably, even as we intellectually (19) ______ that images are easily faked, our biological brains still instinctively react to a photograph with the visceral physiological response reserved for experiencing (20) ______ reality.
