## 📖 Text A: UDHR Extract — Article 25 (adapted) (Questions 1–5)

*"Everyone has the right to a standard of living adequate for the health and well-being of himself and of his family, including food, clothing, housing and medical care and necessary social services, and the right to security in the event of unemployment, sickness, disability, widowhood, old age or other lack of livelihood in circumstances beyond his control."*

**Options:**

## Question 3 — **CEFR: B2** | Difficulty: 7
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-human-rights-001-CEFR-v3`
**Enunciado:** A lawyer argues that if governments do not provide affordable housing, they violate Article 25. A government replies: "Article 25 says everyone has the RIGHT to housing, not that governments must PROVIDE it." What is the key distinction being made?

**Options:**
- [x] A) The distinction between negative rights (obligation NOT to interfere) and positive rights (obligation TO provide) — governmental obligations under positive rights are contested, and governments frequently argue for negative interpretations to limit fiscal obligations, even when the right implies positive duties.
- [ ] B) The government is correct because the word "provide" does not appear anywhere in Article 25.
- [ ] C) All rights in the UDHR automatically impose positive obligations on governments.
- [ ] D) The lawyer is correct — human rights are always positive rights requiring action.

## 📖 Text B: Case Study — Berta Cáceres (abbreviated) (Questions 4–10)

*Berta Cáceres was a Lenca indigenous activist from Honduras who led a successful campaign against the Agua Zarca hydropower project on the Gualcarque River — a site of sacred cultural and ecological significance to the Lenca people. The project had been approved without the Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) of the indigenous communities, in violation of International Labour Organization Convention 169, to which Honduras is a signatory.*

*In 2015, Cáceres was awarded the Goldman Environmental Prize, the most prestigious environmental honor, for her exceptional grassroots organizing. In March 2016, she was murdered at her home. Investigations linked her assassination to employees of the company building the dam, with subsequent evidence connecting corporate officers to the planning of the crime.*

*Her case has become emblematic of a global phenomenon: the killing of environmental and human rights defenders. Global Witness documented 1,733 killings of land and environmental defenders between 2012 and 2022 — the deadliest regions being Latin America, with Colombia as among the most dangerous countries for defenders.*

## 📖 Text C: Personal Testimony — Fictional adaptation (Questions 7–10)

*My name is Camila. I am seventeen years old and I am from Arauca, Colombia. My cousin was a community leader. He organized farmers who were losing their land to large agricultural companies. One morning, men came to his house. We have not seen him since. The police say they cannot find him. International human rights organizations say his case fits the pattern of enforced disappearance.*

*I am speaking through this testimony because someone must. I am afraid every day that the same people who target community leaders will reach my family. But I am also afraid that if no one speaks, no one will know. We are told that speaking up makes us targets. We are also told that silence makes us complicit. There is no safe choice — only choices with different costs.*

## Question 10 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 9
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-human-rights-001-CEFR-v10`
**Enunciado:** Read all three texts (A, B, C) together. What is the central argument they collectively construct about human rights?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Human rights exist at multiple levels simultaneously — as formal legal instruments (UDHR, ILO 169), as contested practices in specific political-economic contexts (Cáceres/Agua Zarca), and as lived negotiations of survival (Camila's testimony) — and the distance between these levels is itself the central human rights problem: rights on paper do not automatically translate into protection in practice.
- [ ] B) Human rights law is completely effective when governments choose to implement it.
- [ ] C) The Latin American context makes human rights discussions irrelevant to the rest of the world.
- [ ] D) Only personal testimony matters in human rights discourse — legal documents are too abstract.

## Question 16 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 9
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-human-rights-001-CEFR-v16`
**Enunciado:** Camila says: "we are told that silence makes us complicit." Unpack this ethical claim. Is it correct? What does complicity mean in a human rights context?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Complicity in human rights discourse can mean benefiting from the conditions a violation creates (e.g., cheaper land prices after a defender disappears) or failing to challenge conditions you could contest. The claim is ethically serious but not universally binding — the ethical weight of silence must be weighed against genuine endangerment. The MORE important claim is that this impossible choice itself constitutes a human rights violation.
- [ ] B) Silence is always complicit in human rights violations — every person has an absolute duty to speak out.
- [ ] C) Complicity only applies to people who directly participate in human rights violations.
- [ ] D) Camila's claim is incorrect — staying silent is always the safer and ethically justified choice for those in danger.
