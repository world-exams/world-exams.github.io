## Reading Text 1: The Myth of Sisyphus (Camus)
The gods had condemned Sisyphus to ceaselessly rolling a rock to the top of a mountain, whence the stone would fall back of its own weight. They had thought with some reason that there is no more dreadful punishment than futile and hopeless labor. If one believes Homer, Sisyphus was the wisest and most prudent of mortals. According to another tradition, however, he was disposed to practice the profession of highwayman. I see no contradiction in this. Opinions differ as to the reasons why he became the futile laborer of the underworld. To begin with, he is accused of a certain levity in regard to the gods. He stole their secrets.

It is during that return, that pause, that Sisyphus interests me. A face that toils so close to stones is already stone itself! I see that man going back down with a heavy yet measured step toward the torment of which he will never know the end. That hour like a breathing-space which returns as surely as his suffering, that is the hour of consciousness. At each of those moments when he leaves the heights and gradually sinks toward the lairs of the gods, he is superior to his fate. He is stronger than his rock. If this myth is tragic, that is because its hero is conscious. Where would his torture be, indeed, if at every step the hope of succeeding upheld him?

I leave Sisyphus at the foot of the mountain! One always finds one's burden again. But Sisyphus teaches the higher fidelity that negates the gods and raises rocks. He too concludes that all is well. This universe henceforth without a master seems to him neither sterile nor futile. Each atom of that stone, each mineral flake of that night-filled mountain, in itself forms a world. The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man's heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy.

## Reading Text 2: The Republic, Book VII (Plato's Cave Paraphrase)
Imagine a number of people living in an underground cave, which has an entrance that opens towards the daylight. The people have been in this dwelling since childhood, shackled by the legs and neck, such that they cannot move and can only look straight ahead of them. Far above and behind them burns a massive fire. Between the fire and the prisoners, there is a parapet, along which puppeteers hold up various objects, statues, and figures of animals made of wood and stone.

The prisoners, unable to turn their heads, see only the shadows of these objects projected onto the wall in front of them. Because they have never seen anything else, they assume these flickering shadows are the absolute, total reality of the world. They even confer honors upon those among them who are fastest at predicting which shadow will appear next.

Now, imagine that one of the prisoners is suddenly freed. He is forced to stand up, turn around, and walk towards the firelight. The sudden glare will cause him profound pain, and he will initially be unable to see the realities of which in his former state he had seen the shadows. If told that his previous life was an illusion and that he is now nearer to reality, he would likely refuse to believe it, fiercely preferring the comfortable, familiar shadows to the blinding, painful truth of the fire, and eventually, the Sun outside the cave.

## Reading Text 3: The Panopticon (Foucault / Bentham)
In the late 18th century, English philosopher Jeremy Bentham designed an architectural concept for a prison called the Panopticon. The design consists of a circular structure with an "inspection house" at its center, from which the manager or staff of the institution can watch the inmates, who are stationed around the perimeter. The crucial architectural ingenuity of the Panopticon is its use of light and blinds: while the watchman in the central tower can see into every single cell, the inmates cannot see into the tower. Thus, an inmate never knows exactly whether they are being watched at any given moment.

In his seminal 1975 work *Discipline and Punish*, French philosopher Michel Foucault seized upon the Panopticon not merely as an architectural curiosity, but as the defining metaphor for modern power. Foucault argued that physical torture and public execution—the hallmarks of sovereign power in the Middle Ages—had been replaced by "disciplinary power." Because the inmate in the Panopticon knows they *might* be watched at an times, they must assume they are being watched at *all* times. Consequently, the external surveillance is internalized. The inmate becomes their own psychological jailer, self-regulating their behavior to conform to the rules without the need for physical force. For Foucault, the genius of the Panopticon is that it creates a state of conscious and permanent visibility that assures the automatic functioning of power.

## Reading Text 4: The Veil of Ignorance (John Rawls)
In his 1971 magnum opus, *A Theory of Justice*, political philosopher John Rawls sought to construct a moral framework for designing a fundamentally just society. Dissatisfied with utilitarianism, which could justify the suffering of a minority if it maximized the happiness of the majority, Rawls revived the social contract tradition with a sweeping thought experiment: the "Original Position." He asked us to imagine a group of individuals tasked with writing the rules, laws, and economic structures of a brand new society.

The critical stipulation of this thought experiment is that these architects must deliberate from behind a "Veil of Ignorance." Behind this veil, no individual knows who they will be in the new society. They are entirely ignorant of their future class, race, gender, intelligence, physical abilities, or even their conception of the "good life." Rawls argued that self-interested, rational actors operating under this absolute uncertainty would inherently design a society that is fair to all. Because anyone *could* be born as a severely disabled, impoverished minority, no rational person would agree to a system that legally oppresses the disabled, impoverished minorities. Therefore, a just society is simply one that you would agree to enter without knowing which societal position you would randomly be placed into.

- [ ] D) Omniscience: The veil actually grants them the magical ability to see the future.

### Explicación Pedagógica
Rawls does *not* rely on people being nice or altruistic. He relies on "self-interested, rational actors." The logic is: "I don't want to suffer, and I *might* be born at the bottom. Therefore, to protect *myself*, I must ensure the bottom is safe." He uses pure selfishness/risk-aversion to artificially manufacture justice. Option B is a perfect philosophical distillation of Rawls's genius mechanic.

### Enunciado
Under Rawls's "Veil of Ignorance," which of the following real-world social policies would the architects ALMOST CERTAINLY agree to implement, despite their self-interested nature?

### Options
- [x] A) A universal minimum standard of healthcare and welfare, ensuring that if they happen to be born sick or poor, they will not be allowed to perish in the streets. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) Extremely strict anti-discrimination laws that aggressively punish racism or sexism, ensuring they aren't subjugated if born as a minority. <!-- weight: 0.6 -->
- [x] C) A heavy inheritance tax on the ultra-rich to fund public schools, ensuring equal opportunity regardless of the parents they are assigned to. <!-- weight: 0.3 -->

### Scoring
- Best answer (A): 1.0 point
- Second best (B): 0.6 points
- Acceptable (C): 0.3 points
- Incorrect (D): 0.0 points

### Explicación Pedagógica
Rawls's philosophy (specifically the "Difference Principle") demands that the worst-off in society must be protected. Since anyone could be the worst-off (sick, poor), Option A (universal safety net against death/suffering) is the most inescapable, primal requirement a self-interested person would demand. Option B (anti-discrimination) is also logically demanded, earning strong points. Option C (egalitarian funding) is a secondary Rawlsian conclusion (equality of opportunity). Option D is the exact opposite of what the veil prevents (no one would agree to a 10% chance of being a slave).
