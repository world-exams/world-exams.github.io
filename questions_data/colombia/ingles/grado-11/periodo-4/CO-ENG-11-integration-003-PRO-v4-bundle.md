## Reading Text 1: The Aesthetics of AI Creation (Part 5 - Inferential)
The recent proliferation of AI image generators has reignited an ancient philosophical debate regarding the definition of art. Historically, art was defined by human *intentionality*; the emotional suffering, physical labor, and deliberate brushstrokes of a human creator were inextricably tied to the value of the final piece. A painting by Van Gogh is revered not merely because it looks pretty, but because it is a desperate, physical manifestation of his tortured psyche.

However, AI generators produce stunning, photorealistic images by scraping billions of human-made data points and mathematically calculating pixel probability. There is no intention, no emotion, no "ghost in the machine"—just a highly advanced autocomplete function reacting to a text prompt. Critics argue that these AI outputs, while visually impressive, are aesthetically hollow; they are "images," but they are not "art." Proponents counter that the human user typing the text prompt is acting as a "curator" or "director," providing the necessary human intentionality to qualify the final image as art, much like an architect directing construction workers to build a cathedral.

## Reading Text 2: The Tragedy of the Commons (Part 6 - Philosophical/Ethical Text)
In 1968, ecologist Garrett Hardin published an influential essay titled *The Tragedy of the Commons*. Hardin asked the reader to picture a common pasture shared by multiple cattle herders. Each individual herder is economically incentivized to maximize their personal gain by adding more cows to their own herd. The herder calculates that the financial benefit of selling one extra cow belongs entirely to them (+1), while the negative consequence of overgrazing that extra cow is shared equally among all the herders (-a fraction).

Because every herder, acting as a rational, self-interested economic agent, independently reaches this exact same mathematical conclusion, they all continuously add cows to the pasture. The inevitable, tragic result is the total destruction of the commons due to overgrazing, meaning all the cows starve and all the herders are ruined. Hardin argued that this paradigm explains modern environmental crises. When the oceans (a global commons) are overfished, or the atmosphere is polluted, individuals and corporations are acting rationally in the short term, but fatally in the long term. Hardin concluded that appealing to a corporation's sense of morality is useless; the tragedy can only be prevented through either strict government coercion (regulation) or total privatization of the resource.
