## 📖 Capstone Text A: "What We Owe Each Other Across Distance" (Questions 1–12)

*In 1971, philosopher John Rawls asked us to imagine designing a society without knowing in advance which position we would occupy within it — a "veil of ignorance" behind which we cannot know whether we will be born rich or poor, healthy or ill, in a powerful or a vulnerable nation. Rawls argued that rational, self-interested agents behind this veil would choose principles that protect the most vulnerable, because any one of them might be that vulnerable person.*

*The thought experiment was designed for societies. But in a globally interconnected world — where a corporation's emission policies in one country determine flooding patterns in another, where pharmaceutical patents set by wealthy nations determine access to medicines in lower-income countries — it becomes increasingly difficult to argue that the veil of ignorance should stop at national borders.*

*Peter Singer's less philosophical and more uncomfortable version of the same argument goes: if you can prevent something very bad from happening without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, you should. The drowning child in the pond is saved by the passerby whether or not they know the child. Distance, Singer argues, changes nothing morally — it only changes our psychology.*

*What neither Rawls nor Singer fully resolves is the question of institutions. Individual moral reasoning is necessary but insufficient. Structural mechanisms — international law, enforceable rights frameworks, redistributive institutions — are the only way to make moral responsibility scalable. The alternative is moral sentiment without architecture.*

## Question 9 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 8
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-capstone-001-CEFR-v9`

**Scenario:** A tech company announces: "Our AI will create 50,000 jobs in Colombia over 5 years, while our systems eliminate 80,000 existing jobs in services and administration."

**Enunciado:** A government spokesperson says: "The net result is economic development — Colombia should welcome this investment." Apply the analytical frameworks from P4 (Future of Work) to evaluate this claim:

**Options:**
- [x] A) The framing commits two analytical errors identified in P4: (1) it treats net aggregate as equivalent to distributed outcome — 50,000 new jobs at different skill levels, locations, and wages for different people than the 80,000 displaced; (2) it ignores timeliness — transition disruption is a real human cost. The claim is a "frictionless transition" fallacy applied at national scale.
- [ ] B) The government is correct — more jobs overall means economic development for everyone.
- [ ] C) The claim is completely false because AI always eliminates more jobs than it creates.
- [ ] D) This scenario cannot be evaluated without knowing exactly which jobs are created and destroyed.
