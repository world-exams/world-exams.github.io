## Reading Text 1: The Trolley Problem and Autonomous Sentience
The classic "Trolley Problem," formulated by Philippa Foot in 1967, asks whether it is morally permissible to steer a runaway trolley onto a side track where it will kill one person, in order to save five people on the main track. For decades, this was merely a philosophical parlor game designed to contrast Utilitarianism (which says killing the one to save the five is mathematically correct) against Deontology (which argues that the active *action* of killing an innocent person is inherently wrong, regardless of the calculation).

Today, however, the Trolley Problem has moved from the philosophy classroom to the engineering laboratory. Manufacturers of autonomous vehicles (AVs) must program the ethical framework of their cars' AI long before the cars hit the road. If an AV’s brakes fail and it is barreling towards a crowd of five pedestrians, should the car actively swerve into a concrete wall, thereby sacrificing the single occupant—its owner—to save the crowd?

Complicating matters further is the "black box" nature of deep learning algorithms. Modern AI does not operate on simple "if-then" rules written by human programmers; it learns through massive data exposure, creating decision-making pathways so complex that even its creators cannot fully explain *why* it made a specific choice. Thus, we are faced with an unprecedented moral crisis: we are delegating life-and-death ethical calculations to a statistical matrix that lacks consciousness, empathy, or moral reasoning, and we cannot cross-examine it when it chooses who lives and who dies. The ethics of the future are not being written by philosophers, but generated invisibly by server farms.

## Reading Text 2: The Right to Be Forgotten
In the digital age, human memory is increasingly outsourced to the infinite, unforgiving archive of the internet. A drunken mistake at age nineteen, a poorly phrased social media post, or a minor criminal charge that was eventually dropped—all are crystallized in the amber of search engine results, permanently accessible to future employers, romantic partners, and the general public. In response to this permanent digital branding, the European Union implemented the "Right to Be Forgotten" (RTBF) in 2014, allowing individuals to request that search engines delist links containing personal information that is "inadequate, irrelevant, or no longer relevant."

Advocates argue that RTBF is a fundamental human right, rooted in the concept of rehabilitation and the freedom of personal evolution. They argue that humans are dynamic entities capable of growth, and it is cruel and unusual punishment to bind a person eternally to the worst moment of their past. However, digital archivists and free speech absolutists vehemently oppose RTBF. They argue that scrubbing search results is a sanitized form of censorship—a digital burning of the books. By allowing individuals, particularly politicians or corporate executives, to erase unfavorable history, society loses its objective historical record. The internet, they argue, must remain an impartial, unredacted library, even if that means preserving the ugly truths of individual lives.

## Reading Text 3: The Bioethics of Genetic Enhancement
The advent of CRISPR-Cas9 gene-editing technology has revolutionized biological science, allowing researchers to cut and paste DNA sequences with unprecedented precision. Currently, the medical consensus restricts the use of CRISPR to "somatic" gene therapy—modifying the genes of an existing patient to cure severe diseases like sickle cell anemia. These modifications affect only the patient and are not passed down to future generations. However, the ethical battleground centers on "germline" editing: modifying the DNA of human embryos.

Proponents of germline editing argue from a position of moral obligation. If we possess the technology to permanently eradicate genetic diseases like cystic fibrosis or Huntington’s from the human gene pool, it would be unethical *not* to use it to prevent future suffering. Opponents, however, warn of a slippery slope from therapeutic prevention to elective enhancement. If we permit the editing of embryos to prevent disease, what prevents wealthy parents from purchasing genomic upgrades for their children—enhancing intelligence, height, or athletic ability? This scenario threatens to create an unprecedented biological caste system, where socioeconomic inequality is permanently hardwired into human DNA. If only the rich can afford to engineer "superior" babies, the egalitarian concept of human rights, predicated on the idea that all humans are created biologically equal, may shatter entirely.

## Reading Text 4: The Ethics of Eating (Peter Singer)
One of the most provocative arguments in modern bioethics is Peter Singer's case for animal liberation. Singer argues from a strict utilitarian perspective: morality requires us to maximize well-being and minimize suffering. Crucially, Singer asserts that the boundary of moral consideration should not be drawn based on species (which he calls "speciesism"), intelligence, or the ability to use complex language. Instead, the only relevant criterion for being included in the moral sphere is *sentience*—the capacity to suffer or feel pain.

Singer observes that modern factory farming inflicts unimaginable, profound physical and psychological suffering on billions of highly sentient creatures (cows, pigs, chickens) every year. Because these animals have central nervous systems and unambiguously experience pain, terror, and despair, their suffering must be factored into our ethical calculations. Singer argues that the minor human pleasure derived from the taste of a hamburger cannot possibly outweigh the immense suffering inflicted upon the cow to produce it. To prioritize human taste over animal torture simply because the animal belongs to a different species is, according to Singer, an arbitrary prejudice logically identical to racism or sexism. He concludes that participating in factory farming by eating meat is an active, profound moral failure.

- [ ] D) Testing cosmetics on mice by putting chemicals in their eyes.

### Explicación Pedagógica
If the *only* rule is "does it have the capacity to suffer?", then anything that *cannot* suffer is outside the moral sphere. A carrot and a tree lack a central nervous system; they are not sentient. Therefore, eating a carrot causes zero suffering, making it morally neutral/permissible under Singer's strict pain metric. Options A, B, and D all involve harming or killing creatures with central nervous systems (sentience).

### Enunciado
If both Peter Singer (Text 4) and Philippa Foot (The Trolley Problem, Text 1) are discussing Utilitarianism, which of the following statements creates the most accurate synthesis of the two texts regarding this philosophy?

### Options
- [x] A) Utilitarianism is a strictly mathematical ethics (maximizing good, minimizing bad); Singer uses this math to include animals (burger taste < animal torture), while the Trolley Problem uses it to justify sacrificing humans (1 death < 5 deaths). <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) Both texts prove that Deontology is the only correct philosophy because Utilitarianism always requires torturing animals and killing people on train tracks. <!-- weight: 0.6 -->
- [x] C) The Trolley problem proves that machines can use Utilitarianism, while Singer proves that only humans can use it. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->
- [ ] D) Both authors agree that we should stop driving cars and start eating meat to balance the universe. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring
- Best answer (A): 1.0 point
- Acceptable (B): 0.6 points
- Incorrect (C, D): 0.0 points

### Explicación Pedagógica
Utilitarianism is the mathematical calculation of outcome (greatest good/least suffering for the greatest number). Text 1 shows how it dictates steering the trolley to crush 1 person to save 5 (net +4 lives). Text 4 shows how Singer expands this math to animals: the taste of a burger (+1 pleasure) does not justify the torture of a cow (-1000 suffering), so it is a net moral negative. Option A flawlessly synthesizes how the exact same philosophy operates in both texts. Option B is a cynical but arguable critique of utilitarianism. Options C and D are nonsensical readings.
