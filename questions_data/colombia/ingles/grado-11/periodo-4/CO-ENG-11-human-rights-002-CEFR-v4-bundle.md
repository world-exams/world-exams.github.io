## Question 6 — **CEFR: B2** | Difficulty: 7
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-human-rights-002-CEFR-v6`

**Enunciado:** Rights language often uses "ensure," "guarantee," "respect," and "protect." What is the distinction between "protect" and "respect" in formal rights discourse?

**Options:**
- [x] A) "Respect" implies a negative duty — the state should not violate the right; "protect" implies a positive duty — the state should prevent third parties (corporations, individuals) from violating it. Both are part of the standard tripartite obligation (respect, protect, fulfill) in international human rights law.
- [ ] B) "Protect" and "respect" are interchangeable in all legal contexts.
- [ ] C) "Protect" is stronger — it implies legal punishment; "respect" is weaker — just moral acknowledgment.
- [ ] D) "Respect" applies to social rights and "protect" to civil and political rights only.

## Question 17 — **CEFR: C1** | Difficulty: 9
**ID:** `CO-ENG-11-human-rights-002-CEFR-v17`

**Enunciado:** Distinguish between these formal terms commonly confused in human rights writing: "violation," "infringement," "breach."

**Options:**
- [x] A) "Violation" is broad — any failure to comply with rights; "infringement" often implies partial or incremental limitation of a right (more graduated); "breach" is typically used for contractual or treaty obligations specifically (breach of treaty, breach of duty). In practice, overlap exists but "violation" is the most commonly used in human rights discourse.
- [ ] B) All three terms are completely interchangeable in human rights writing.
- [ ] C) "Breach" is the strongest term, "infringement" is moderate, and "violation" is the weakest.
- [ ] D) "Violation" applies only to human rights; "infringement" only to intellectual property; "breach" only to contracts.
