## 📖 Text A: "The Trolley Problem and Its Limits" (Questions 1–8)

*The trolley problem, first formulated by philosopher Philippa Foot and later developed by Judith Jarvis Thomson, asks: a runaway trolley is heading toward five people tied to the tracks. You can divert it to a side track where only one person is tied. Do you pull the lever?*

*Most people, when surveyed, say yes. The arithmetic seems clear: one death is better than five. This is straightforward consequentialism — the right action is the one that produces the greatest good for the greatest number.*

*But Thomson complicated the scenario. Suppose instead that you are on a bridge above the tracks, and the only way to stop the trolley and save the five is to push a large man off the bridge — his body will stop the trolley. Most people say no to this version, even though the arithmetic is identical: one death to save five.*

*The difference reveals something important about moral intuition: we appear to distinguish between killing someone as a side effect of achieving a good outcome and killing someone as a means to that outcome. Deontological ethics — the view that some actions are inherently wrong regardless of their consequences — captures this intuition: it is never permissible to use a person merely as a means to an end, even a good end.*

*Neither framework provides a complete answer. Consequentialism risks licensing terrible acts for sufficiently large benefits. Deontology risks paralysis in emergencies where not acting is itself harmful.*

## 📖 Text B: "A Short Introduction to Logical Fallacies" (Questions 6–13)

*Critical thinking requires not just understanding good arguments but recognizing bad ones. Three of the most common fallacies in public discourse:*

*1. **Ad Hominem** (attack the person): Dismissing an argument by attacking the speaker rather than the argument itself. "You can't trust her analysis of climate policy — she drives a car." Even if she drives a car, this is irrelevant to whether her analysis is correct.*

*2. **Straw Man**: Misrepresenting your opponent's position to make it easier to attack. "The scientists want to abolish all cars immediately." In fact, they recommended a phased transition over 20 years. Attacking the distorted version is easier but intellectually dishonest.*

*3. **False Dilemma**: Presenting a situation as having only two options when more exist. "Either we stop all immigration or crime will continue to rise." This ignores the possibility that immigration policy and crime rates are not causally linked and that many policy options exist.*

## Question 10 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 9
**Enunciado:** Connect the trolley problem (Text A) to fallacy detection (Text B). How does understanding ethical frameworks help identify logical fallacies?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Ethical frameworks clarify the TYPE of consideration being made (consequences vs. principles), which reveals when an argument illegitimately conflates them — for example, using a deontological argument against a consequentialist policy without acknowledging you are changing the evaluative framework is a kind of informal fallacy (equivocation on standards). ## Question 15 — **CEFR: B2** | Difficulty: 6
**Enunciado:** "All taxes are theft" is a claim sometimes made in political discourse. Which type of fallacy might this contain?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Equivocation — using "theft" (which in normal usage implies illegal, unconsented taking of property) to describe taxation (a coercive but legally mandated social contract mechanism), the argument forces acceptance of an ideological position by smuggling it into a definitional claim, rather than making an argument.
- [ ] B) Ad hominem — attacking those who support taxation personally.
- [ ] C) Straw man — misrepresenting the position of pro-tax economists.
- [ ] D) This is not a fallacy — it is an accurate description of what taxes do.

## Question 16 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 8
**Enunciado:** A student argues in class: "My parents and most people in my community oppose this policy, so it must be wrong." What fallacy is this?

**Options:**
- [x] A) Appeal to popularity / ad populum — the wrongness or rightness of a policy cannot be determined by how many people oppose it, only by argument and evidence; consensus (especially local consensus) is not evidence for moral truth.
- [ ] B) False dilemma — presenting only two positions when more exist.
- [ ] C) Ad hominem — attacking the policy-makers personally.
- [ ] D) Straw man — misrepresenting the official policy.

## Question 17 — **CEFR: B2+** | Difficulty: 9

- [x] A) This conflates individual moral responsibility (the current generation did not commit historical crimes) with collective institutional responsibility (current institutions, laws, wealth distributions, and land arrangements bear the effects of historical injustice, and remedying them is about rectifying ongoing structural effects, not punishing individuals for ancestral actions). - [ ] B) The argument is fully valid — moral responsibility requires personal participation in the wrong.
- [ ] C) The argument is completely invalid — current generations inherit both benefits AND moral liabilities of their predecessors without qualification.
- [ ] D) The argument is valid in liberal democracies but not in post-colonial states.
