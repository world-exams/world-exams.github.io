# Bundle: Future of Work & AI — Reading Articles on Automation and the Skills Economy
**CEFR Level:** B2 (Questions range B1+ → C1)
**Focus:** Reading non-fiction articles on AI, automation, and future employment; identifying arguments, evaluating evidence, synthesis with real-world context

---

## 📖 Text A: "The Skills Paradox" (Questions 1–10)

*The most frequently invoked response to automation anxiety is "upskilling" — the proposition that workers displaced by technology will find new careers by learning new capabilities. The argument has the virtue of optimism: it implies that the problem is solvable, the solution is within individuals' control, and the responsibility is distributed across the market rather than concentrated in any single institution.*

*But the arithmetic of upskilling has been less examined than its rhetoric. Research by the McKinsey Global Institute estimates that up to 375 million workers globally may need to change occupation by 2030 as a result of automation. At the same time, corporate investment in training has fallen by 28% per employee over the last twenty years, according to MIT economists. The gap between the number of workers who need to be reskilled and the resources devoted to reskilling them has never been wider.*

*This is the skills paradox: the moment when upskilling is most urgently needed is precisely the moment when it is least economically incentivized. Companies facing short-term competitive pressure cut training budgets. Workers in precarious employment lack the time and resources to invest in skills development. The resulting system produces a discourse of individual responsibility for structural outcomes.*

## 📖 Text B: "What AI Really Does to Jobs" (Questions 6–13)

*A common error in public discussions of AI and employment is treating automation as synonymous with job destruction. Historical evidence suggests a more complicated story: the mechanization of agriculture in the 20th century did not eliminate labor — it restructured it, displacing agricultural workers into manufacturing, then service sectors. Similarly, ATMs did not eliminate bank tellers; research found that ATMs reduced the cost of running bank branches, which allowed banks to open more branches, which actually increased teller employment — though with a shift in job function toward customer service rather than cash handling.*

*The question is not whether AI destroys jobs in absolute terms but whether the restructuring it produces generates accessible employment for those it displaces, at comparable wages, in a timely manner. On all three dimensions, current evidence is mixed. The new jobs created by AI-related industries are often high-skill and geographically concentrated; the displaced jobs are often low-skill and geographically dispersed. The transition is rarely frictionless.*

*Moreover, the pace of this transition matters enormously. Historical labor transitions unfolded over decades; today's AI-driven changes may concentrate restructuring into years. Even if the long-run outcome is positive employment equilibrium, the short-run social cost — in unemployment, poverty, and community disruption — is a policy problem of the first order.*
