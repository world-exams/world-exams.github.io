# Bundle: World Cultures & Global Interconnection (Integrated P5 & P6)

This final bundle of Grade 10 English integrates literal (Part 5, Q1-10) and inferential (Part 6, Q11-20) reading comprehension in the topic of world cultural exchange and globalization. It closes the Grade 10 series at a challenging B2 level.

---

## Master Context: Globalization — A Cultural Roulette? (Questions 1-20)

Globalization has connected humanity in unprecedented ways. Ideas, food, music, and fashion travel across borders with extraordinary speed, creating a rich, hybrid global culture. A teenager in Bogotá can listen to K-Pop, eat a Japanese ramen bowl for lunch, and then code in American English — all in one day.

However, critics argue this global cultural exchange is not a level playing field. Dominant economies — particularly the United States — export their culture far more effectively than smaller nations can. Hollywood films, American fast food, and English-language internet content set the global standard, pushing local languages and cultural expressions to the margins.

This process is called "cultural homogenization" — the tendency for cultures worldwide to become increasingly similar under the influence of dominant global powers. Opponents of homogenization argue that it erodes linguistic diversity, makes local art markets uncompetitive, and creates a sense of "rootlessness" among young people who feel disconnected from their own heritage.

Defenders counter that cultural exchange is not the same as cultural destruction. Instead, they argue, exposure to global culture can actually reinvigorate local creativity, which often expresses itself in inventive fusions — like the cumbia-electronic music scene in Colombia, or the K-drama fans in Mexico who are now learning Korean. They argue that cultures are not fragile objects to be protected in museums, but rather living, breathing systems capable of adapting and evolving.
