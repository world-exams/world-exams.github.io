## Master Context A: What is Digital Citizenship? (Questions 1-10)

Digital citizenship refers to the responsible and ethical use of technology, particularly the internet. A good digital citizen understands how to protect their personal data, engage respectfully in online communities, and evaluate information sources critically.

Being a digital citizen also means understanding your digital footprint — the trail of data you leave behind every time you browse online, post on social media, or use an app. This footprint is permanent and can affect your academic and professional future. Employers and universities increasingly check candidates' online presence.

Schools across Colombia are now integrating digital citizenship into their curricula, teaching students not only how to use technology but also how to use it responsibly. Among the key topics covered are cyberbullying prevention, online privacy, and media literacy.

## Master Context B: Online Privacy & Data Rights (Questions 11-20)

When you sign up for a social media platform, you agree to a Terms of Service that grants the company significant rights over your data. Most users never read these documents, which can be dozens of pages long. As a result, they unknowingly consent to their information — including location, preferences, and even private messages — being used for targeted advertising.

Data protection laws, such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in Europe, give citizens the right to access, correct, and delete their personal data. Colombia has its own data protection law, known as Habeas Data (Law 1581 of 2012), which provides similar protections for its citizens.

Experts recommend that young people review privacy settings regularly, avoid sharing sensitive personal information on public platforms, and use strong, unique passwords for every account. A single data breach can expose millions of records.
