This bundle focuses on Part 6: inferential reading comprehension. Students must go beyond the text to deduce implications, evaluate arguments, and understand the author's purpose. The topic is Artificial Intelligence ethics at a B2 level.

## Master Context A: Can Machines Make Fair Decisions? (Questions 1-10)

Artificial Intelligence is increasingly being used to make high-stakes decisions: who receives a bank loan, which prisoner gets early parole, or which job candidate advances to the next round. Advocates argue that AI removes human bias from these decisions because machines simply follow the data. Critics, however, point out that this logic has a fatal flaw: the data itself often reflects historical injustices.

Consider a system trained on decades of past criminal sentencing data. If historically certain communities were sentenced more harshly due to social prejudice, the AI will learn to associate those communities with higher risk — not because they are inherently more dangerous, but because the data reflects a biased justice system. The AI then perpetuates this bias at scale, affecting thousands of people at once.

The concept of "explainability" is central to the debate. Many modern AI models, particularly deep learning systems, operate as "black boxes" — their internal decision-making process is not transparent even to their developers. If a machine denies you a loan, you have the right to know why. But if the AI cannot explain itself, this right becomes meaningless.

## Master Context B: Who is Responsible for AI's Mistakes? (Questions 11-20)

When an autonomous vehicle crashes, who is liable — the driver, the manufacturer, or the AI developer? When a medical AI misdiagnoses a patient, is the doctor responsible for trusting it? As AI becomes embedded in critical systems, questions of legal and moral responsibility become increasingly urgent and complex.

Current legal frameworks were not designed with AI in mind. Most laws assume a human agent is ultimately responsible for any action. AI challenges this assumption. Some legal scholars propose the creation of a new category of "electronic personhood" — a legal status for AI systems that would allow them to hold some form of responsibility. Others strongly oppose this, arguing it would allow human actors (developers, companies) to escape accountability by hiding behind their machines.

Meanwhile, a "human-in-the-loop" philosophy is gaining traction. This approach insists that a human must always be the final decision-maker in any critical situation, with AI serving only in an advisory capacity. Proponents argue this preserves human accountability, while critics counter that it defeats the supposed efficiency gains of automation in time-sensitive contexts.
