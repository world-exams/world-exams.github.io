## Master Context A: How Algorithms Shape Your Reality (Questions 1-10)

You may not see them, but algorithms are constantly working in the background of your digital life. Every time a streaming service recommends a movie, a social media platform decides what post you see first, or a search engine ranks its results, an algorithm has made that decision.

An algorithm is simply a set of rules or instructions that a computer follows to solve a problem or complete a task. However, when these algorithms are trained on biased data, they can perpetuate and even amplify human prejudices. For example, hiring algorithms have been found to favor certain demographic profiles over others, not because they were programmed to discriminate, but because they learned from historical hiring patterns that were themselves biased.

Critics argue that the transparency of these systems is essential. If we cannot understand how an algorithmic decision was made, we cannot challenge it. This has led to new demands for "algorithmic accountability" — the idea that companies must be able to explain and justify the decisions their automated systems make.

## Master Context B: The Filter Bubble Problem (Questions 11-20)

Social media algorithms are designed to maximize "engagement" — the amount of time you spend on a platform. To achieve this, they learn your preferences and show you content that you are likely to agree with, click on, or react to emotionally. The result is a "filter bubble."

Inside a filter bubble, users are exposed to a narrow, personalized stream of information that reinforces their existing beliefs. This can be pleasant — you see things you like — but it has serious consequences for how people understand the world. Research suggests that filter bubbles contribute to political polarization, as people in different bubbles develop radically different ideas about what is true.

Some platforms have begun experimenting with "diverse feed" options, which deliberately expose users to different viewpoints. However, many users actively choose to return to their familiar bubble, suggesting that the challenge is as much psychological as it is technological.
