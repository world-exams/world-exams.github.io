# Bundle: Social Media & Identity (ICFES Part 5 - Literal Reading)

This bundle focuses on Part 5: literal reading comprehension. The topic is how social media shapes personal identity, requiring students to find explicit information within analytical texts at a B1+/B2 level.

---

## Master Context A: The Performance of Identity Online (Questions 1-10)

Social media has fundamentally changed how young people construct and present their identities. On platforms like Instagram and TikTok, users curate their public persona — carefully selecting photos, captions, and hashtags to create a specific impression. Researchers call this "identity performance."

Studies show that teenagers who heavily invest in their online persona often experience a phenomenon known as "impression management fatigue" — the exhaustion that comes from constantly maintaining a polished digital image. This fatigue can contribute to anxiety, low self-esteem when receiving fewer likes than expected, and a disconnection between online and offline selves.

However, social media also offers positive possibilities for identity exploration. LGBTQ+ youth, for instance, often find online communities to be safe spaces where they can explore their identities before doing so in the physical world. Similarly, students from minority groups can find communities that celebrate their cultural heritage, providing a sense of belonging that might be absent in their immediate environment.

## Master Context B: The Attention Economy (Questions 11-20)

Social media platforms are not designed to make you happy — they are designed to capture and hold your attention, which they then sell to advertisers. This model is known as the "attention economy." Every feature of these platforms, from infinite scrolling to notification alerts, is engineered to keep users engaged as long as possible.

Former engineers from major tech companies have publicly admitted that these features are deliberately addictive. They compare them to slot machines: you never know what you're going to get when you pull the lever (or scroll down), and this unpredictability is what keeps you coming back.

The consequences of this design philosophy are significant. Studies have linked heavy social media use to decreased attention spans, disrupted sleep patterns, and increased rates of anxiety and depression, particularly among adolescents. Some experts are calling for greater regulation of these platforms, similar to how tobacco advertising is regulated, arguing that their products are harmful to public health.
