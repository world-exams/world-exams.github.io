## Master Context: The Anatomy of a Cyberattack (Questions 1-20)

Every day, millions of cyberattacks occur around the world. These range from simple phishing emails — messages designed to trick people into revealing passwords — to sophisticated ransomware campaigns that can cripple entire hospital systems or government agencies.

One of the most common entry points for attackers is the human element. Studies show that over 80% of data breaches involve some form of "social engineering" — manipulating people rather than hacking software. A hacker might impersonate an IT technician, send an urgent message from a "CEO," or create a fake login page that looks identical to a bank's real one.

Protecting against these threats requires both technical and behavioral change. At the technical level, two-factor authentication (2FA), encrypted communications, and regular software updates significantly reduce vulnerabilities. At the behavioral level, being skeptical of unsolicited requests, verifying identities before sharing information, and using a password manager are fundamental habits.

The consequences of cybercrime extend beyond individuals. Attacks on critical infrastructure — power grids, water systems, financial networks — have the potential to disrupt entire societies. Security experts warn that as our world becomes increasingly connected through the Internet of Things (IoT), the attack surface grows larger. Every connected device, from a smart refrigerator to an industrial sensor, is a potential entry point for a malicious actor.
