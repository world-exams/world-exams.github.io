## Question 7 (Difícil A - Difficulty 4)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-media-001-PRO-v7`
**Type:** `single`
**ICFES:** Comunicativa
**Bloom:** Analyze

### Contexto
Read the following situation:

"A viral video shows a polar bear walking through a city street. The caption reads: 'Climate change is forcing wild animals into our cities!' The video receives 2 million shares. Later, it is revealed that the bear escaped from a nearby zoo."

### Enunciado
What does this situation illustrate about how **misinformation spreads** on social media?

### Options
- [x] A) People share emotional content quickly without verifying the context, and misleading captions can distort the meaning of real footage.
- [ ] B) Social media algorithms always verify the accuracy of content before allowing it to go viral.
- [ ] C) Videos cannot be used to spread misinformation because they show real events.
- [ ] D) Only professional journalists share misleading information online.

### Explicación Pedagógica
Este caso ilustra cómo **la desinformación se propaga**: el video es real (un oso en la calle), pero el contexto es falso (no es por cambio climático, sino porque escapó del zoo). Las emociones impulsan el compartir sin verificar.

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-media-001-PRO-v8`

**Bloom:** Analyze

### Contexto
Identifying passive voice in news reporting.

**Sentence A:** "The mayor closed the school due to safety concerns."
**Sentence B:** "The school was closed due to safety concerns."

### Enunciado
Why do journalists sometimes prefer **Sentence B (passive voice)** over Sentence A in news reports?

### Options
- [x] A) Passive voice allows the writer to focus on the action/event rather than the person responsible, which can make reporting seem more neutral or conceal responsibility.
- [ ] B) Passive voice is always grammatically incorrect and should never be used in writing.
- [ ] C) Passive voice makes sentences shorter and easier to understand.
- [ ] D) Passive voice is used only when the writer does not know English well.

### Explicación Pedagógica
La **voz pasiva** en periodismo tiene funciones estratégicas: puede enfocarse en el evento ("the school was closed") en vez del agente ("the mayor"), creando neutralidad aparente o incluso ocultando responsabilidad. Analizar esta elección gramatical es competencia avanzada.

What is **misleading** about this advertisement?

## Question 12 (Muy Difícil C - Difficulty 5)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-media-001-PRO-v12`
**Type:** `single`
**ICFES:** Gramatical + Comunicativa
**Bloom:** Evaluate

### Contexto
Identifying hedging language in academic vs. sensationalist writing.

**Text A (Academic):** "The evidence *suggests* that social media *may* contribute to increased anxiety levels in some adolescents."
**Text B (Sensationalist):** "Social media *CAUSES* anxiety in ALL teenagers!"

### Enunciado
Why is the **hedging language** in Text A ("suggests," "may," "some") more appropriate for reporting scientific findings?

### Options
- [x] A) Scientific research acknowledges uncertainty and limitations; hedging language accurately reflects that the evidence shows correlation, not definitive causation for all individuals.
- [ ] B) Scientists use vague language because they don't understand their own research well.
- [ ] C) Hedging makes the text longer and more impressive to academic readers.
- [ ] D) There is no difference — both texts convey the same meaning equally.

### Explicación Pedagógica
El **lenguaje hedging** (atenuador) usando "suggests," "may," "some" es fundamental en escritura académica porque refleja la naturaleza tentativa de la evidencia científica. Text B comete la falacia de presentar correlación como causalidad universal.

## Question 14 (Muy Difícil E - Difficulty 5)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-media-001-PRO-v14`
**Type:** `single`
**ICFES:** Comunicativa + Lexical
**Bloom:** Evaluate

### Contexto
Analyzing framing in news headlines.

The same event is reported differently by two outlets:
- **Outlet A:** "Protesters clash with police during demonstration"
- **Outlet B:** "Police use excessive force against peaceful protesters"

### Enunciado
How does the **framing** differ between these two headlines?

### Options
- [x] A) Outlet A frames the situation as mutual conflict ("clash"), while Outlet B assigns blame to police ("excessive force") and portrays protesters as victims ("peaceful").
- [ ] B) Both headlines describe exactly the same event in exactly the same way.
- [ ] C) Outlet A is always more accurate because it mentions both sides.
- [ ] D) Outlet B is always more accurate because it defends the protesters.

### Explicación Pedagógica

"When a natural disaster strikes, social media becomes both a lifeline and a minefield. During the 2023 Turkish earthquake, Twitter helped coordinate rescue efforts and connect families — volunteers used hashtags to locate survivors. The same platform that saved lives also exploited the crisis for profit and political manipulation."

### Enunciado

### Options

- Each wrong selection: -0.5

### Explicación Pedagógica

## Question 19 (Razonamiento Abstracto - Difficulty 9)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-media-001-PRO-v19`
**Type:** `weighted`
**ICFES:** Comunicativa + Gramatical + Pragmática
**Bloom:** Meta-cognitive

### Contexto
Consider this thought experiment:

"Imagine a perfect fact-checking AI that can instantly verify any claim as TRUE or FALSE with 100% accuracy. Would misinformation disappear? Consider: even today, people often KNOW that something is false but believe it anyway because it aligns with their identity, community, or worldview. Studies show that presenting people with corrective facts often makes them believe the false claim MORE strongly — a phenomenon called the **'backfire effect.'**"

### Enunciado
What does the backfire effect reveal about the **fundamental nature** of misinformation?

### Options
- [x] A) Misinformation is not primarily a knowledge problem but an identity problem — people don't believe false things because they lack facts, but because those beliefs serve psychological and social needs (belonging, meaning, control); therefore, even perfect fact-checking cannot solve it. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) The backfire effect shows that belief and knowledge are different psychological systems — you can know something is false and still believe it, which means combating misinformation requires addressing emotions and identity, not just providing facts. <!-- weight: 0.8 -->
- [x] C) The fact-checking AI would help some people but not everyone, because some people are stubborn. <!-- weight: 0.3 -->
- [ ] D) The backfire effect doesn't exist — people always accept facts when presented with clear evidence. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring
- A: 1.0 (identifies the paradigm shift from knowledge-deficit to identity-protection model)
- B: 0.8 (correctly identifies belief vs. knowledge distinction)
- C: 0.3 (partially true but superficial — "stubborn" misses the psychological mechanism)
- D: 0.0 (denies the empirically documented phenomenon)

### Explicación Pedagógica
Esta pregunta demanda **meta-cognición**: pensar sobre cómo pensamos. El insight clave es que la desinformación no es un problema de acceso a datos sino de **identidad psicológica**: las creencias falsas persisten porque satisfacen necesidades emocionales (pertenencia, sentido de control). Este análisis distingue pensadores profundos de superficiales.

## Question 20 (Transferencia Extraordinaria - Difficulty 10)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-media-001-PRO-v20`
**Type:** `weighted`
**ICFES:** Comunicativa + Pragmática + Lexical + Gramatical
**Bloom:** Transfer+

### Contexto
Consider these two phenomena from different fields:

**Immunology:** When a population is exposed to a weakened version of a virus (vaccine), individuals develop antibodies that protect them from the full-strength virus. This principle is called **inoculation**.

**Media studies:** Researchers have found that briefly exposing people to weakened forms of misinformation techniques (e.g., showing them simplified examples of emotional manipulation, ad hominem attacks, or false experts) makes them more resistant to real-world manipulation. This has been called **"psychological inoculation"** or **"prebunking."**

### Enunciado
How does the **biological concept of inoculation** transfer to media literacy, and what does this transfer reveal about the nature of critical thinking?

### Options
- [x] A) Just as biological immune systems need controlled exposure to pathogens to build defenses, critical thinking is not an abstract skill but a practical immune system that must be "trained" through controlled exposure to manipulation techniques; this reveals that media literacy should be taught experientially (practice identifying deception) rather than theoretically (memorizing rules about credible sources). <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) "Prebunking" works like a vaccine for the mind — small doses of misinformation techniques help build mental defenses against larger attacks, suggesting that prevention is more effective than correction. <!-- weight: 0.6 -->
- [x] C) The immune system analogy shows that education can help people resist misinformation. <!-- weight: 0.2 -->
- [ ] D) The comparison is invalid because the human body and the human mind work in completely different ways and cannot be compared. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring
- A: 1.0 (full transfer — extracts the meta-principle AND its pedagogical implication)
- B: 0.6 (correct operational transfer but doesn't extract the deeper insight about experiential learning)
- C: 0.2 (surface-level observation)
- D: 0.0 (rejects analogical thinking)

### Explicación Pedagógica
Esta pregunta evalúa **transferencia extraordinaria**: mapear el mecanismo biológico de la inoculación al dominio cognitivo, y extraer la **implicación pedagógica** (aprendizaje experiencial > teórico). La respuesta A demuestra tres niveles de transferencia: (1) reconocer la analogía, (2) explicar el mecanismo, y (3) derivar implicaciones prácticas. Este tipo de pensamiento analógico multinivel es característico de IQ superior (120+).
