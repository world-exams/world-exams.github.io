## Question 7 (Difícil A - Difficulty 4)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-tech-001-PRO-v7`
**Type:** `single`
**ICFES:** Comunicativa
**Bloom:** Analyze

### Contexto
Read the following data:

"A 2024 survey of 5,000 Colombian students aged 13-17 found that:
- 78% use social media more than 3 hours daily
- 45% report feeling anxious when their phone battery drops below 20%
- 62% check their phone within the first 5 minutes after waking up
- Only 12% can go an entire weekend without their phone"

### Enunciado
What pattern do these statistics collectively suggest about teenagers' relationship with technology?

### Options
- [x] A) The data suggests a high level of technology dependence bordering on behavioral addiction, with physical and emotional symptoms when access is limited.
- [ ] B) The data shows that Colombian teenagers are exceptionally productive because they use technology efficiently.
- [ ] C) The statistics prove that social media has no significant effect on teenage behavior.
- [ ] D) The survey demonstrates that only 12% of teenagers actually enjoy using their phones.

### Explicación Pedagógica
Los datos muestran **patrones de dependencia**: uso extensivo (+3h/día), ansiedad por batería baja, uso compulsivo al despertar, e incapacidad de desconectar. Analizar múltiples datos para identificar un patrón conjunto es competencia analítica.

### Contexto
Understanding Terms of Service agreements.

"By using this app, you agree to share your location data, contact list, browsing history, and microphone access with our partners for 'service improvement purposes.'"

### Enunciado
What is the **ethical concern** with this Terms of Service agreement?

### Options
- [x] A) The broad data collection (location, contacts, browsing, microphone) exceeds what is necessary for the app's function, and vague language ("service improvement") obscures how the data will actually be used.

### Contexto
Evaluating the concept of algorithmic bias.

"When a tech company trained its hiring AI on 10 years of past hiring data, the system learned to favor male candidates because the company had historically hired mostly men for technical roles. The AI replicated and amplified existing human biases."

### Enunciado
What does this case demonstrate about **AI and bias**?

### Options
- [x] A) AI systems are not inherently neutral — they reflect and amplify the biases present in their training data, potentially automating discrimination at scale.
- [ ] B) AI is always more fair than human decision-makers because machines cannot be prejudiced.
- [ ] C) The AI correctly identified that men are better at technical jobs than women.

## Question 19 (Razonamiento Abstracto - Difficulty 9)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-tech-001-PRO-v19`
**Type:** `weighted`
**ICFES:** Comunicativa + Gramatical + Pragmática
**Bloom:** Meta-cognitive

### Contexto
Consider this thought experiment:

"A social media platform creates an AI that perfectly detects and removes ALL false information in real time. However, to achieve this, the AI must read and analyze every private message, every draft, and every thought shared by every user. The AI is accurate 99.99% of the time, but in 0.01% of cases, it mistakenly removes true information that it misclassifies as false."

### Enunciado
What **fundamental trade-off** does this scenario reveal, and why is it impossible to resolve with technology alone?

### Options
- [x] A) It reveals the irreconcilable tension between **truth** and **freedom**: eliminating misinformation requires total surveillance which destroys privacy, and even near-perfect accuracy creates censorship of true information at scale; the trade-off is not technical but philosophical — no algorithm can determine the acceptable ratio of false positives to privacy loss because that is a human values question, not an engineering problem. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) The trade-off is between fighting misinformation and protecting privacy — you can't have perfect truth detection without perfect surveillance, which means society must accept some level of misinformation to preserve freedom. <!-- weight: 0.7 -->
- [x] C) The 0.01% error rate means some true things will be censored, which is a problem. <!-- weight: 0.3 -->
- [ ] D) There is no trade-off — a better AI would achieve 100% accuracy without needing to read private messages. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring
- A: 1.0 (identifies the *philosophical impossibility*, not just the practical challenge)
- B: 0.7 (correct trade-off identified but doesn't address why technology alone can't solve it)
- C: 0.3 (identifies one symptom but misses the structural argument)
- D: 0.0 (techno-solutionist fallacy)

### Explicación Pedagógica
Esta pregunta exige **meta-cognición**: pensar sobre los límites del pensamiento computacional. El insight profundo es que el trade-off verdad/libertad no es un problema de ingeniería (mejor algoritmo) sino **filosófico** (valores en conflicto irreconciliables). Reconocer los límites de la tecnología para resolver problemas humanos es razonamiento de nivel 9.

## Question 20 (Transferencia Extraordinaria - Difficulty 10)

**ID:** `CO-ENG-09-tech-001-PRO-v20`
**Type:** `weighted`
**ICFES:** Comunicativa + Pragmática + Lexical + Gramatical
**Bloom:** Transfer+

### Contexto
Consider these two concepts from different fields:

**Urban Planning — "Desire Paths":** When architects design walking paths in parks, people often ignore them and create their own trails through the grass — called "desire paths." Smart urban planners wait to see where these natural paths form and then pave THOSE instead of pre-designed routes.

**Technology Design — "Dark Patterns":** Tech companies design user interfaces that manipulate users into unintended actions: making the "unsubscribe" button tiny and gray, pre-checking consent boxes, or making "delete account" require 15 steps. These exploit natural human behavior for corporate benefit.

### Enunciado
Both examples show designers responding to human behavior, but in opposite ways. What does comparing "desire paths" (adapting design to users) versus "dark patterns" (exploiting users through design) reveal about the **ethics of design itself**?

### Options
- [x] A) Both concepts demonstrate that design is never neutral — it always embodies a value system; desire paths represent *empathetic design* (adapting to serve the user's natural intent), while dark patterns represent *exploitative design* (manipulating the user's natural behavior against their interests); this reveals that the fundamental ethical question in technology is not "what CAN we build?" but "whose interests does the design serve?" — making design literacy as important as digital literacy. <!-- weight: 1.0 -->
- [x] B) Desire paths show design that respects users, and dark patterns show design that exploits them; the ethical difference is about whether the designer's goal is to serve the user or to extract value from them. <!-- weight: 0.6 -->
- [x] C) Good design should follow what users naturally want to do, and bad design tries to trick them. <!-- weight: 0.2 -->
- [ ] D) The two concepts are unrelated because urban planning and technology are completely different fields with nothing in common. <!-- weight: 0.0 -->

### Scoring
- A: 1.0 (transfers the meta-principle and derives original implications about design literacy)
- B: 0.6 (correct comparison but doesn't extract the meta-principle)
- C: 0.2 (superficially correct but fails to articulate the ethical framework)
- D: 0.0 (rejects the cross-domain transfer)

### Explicación Pedagógica
Esta pregunta exige **transferencia extraordinaria** entre urbanismo y diseño tecnológico. El insight de nivel 10 es que el **diseño nunca es neutral** — siempre incorpora un sistema de valores. La diferencia entre "desire paths" y "dark patterns" es la intención subyacente: servir al usuario vs. explotarlo. Derivar que la "alfabetización del diseño" es tan importante como la alfabetización digital requiere pensamiento analógico multinivel, característica de IQ 130+.
